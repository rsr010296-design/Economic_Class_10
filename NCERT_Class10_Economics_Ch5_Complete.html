Consumer Exploitation in the Marketplace
Why consumers are in a weak position

Consumers participate in the market when they purchase goods and services. Individual consumers are often in a weak position because producers are few and powerful while consumers purchase in small amounts and are scattered.

Forms of exploitation: underweighting, adding hidden charges, selling adulterated/defective goods, false advertising (baby powder milk claimed "better than mother's milk"), suppressing safety information (cigarettes and cancer took years of court battles). Large companies with huge wealth can manipulate markets through media and false information.

The seller's usual attitude: "If you didn't like what you bought, go elsewhere." The consumer movement is an effort to change this situation and shift responsibility to sellers.
Consumer Movement and COPRA
How organised struggle led to legal protection

The consumer movement arose from dissatisfaction with unfair practices and absence of legal protection. In India, food shortages, hoarding, black marketing, and adulteration in the 1960s gave birth to organised consumer movement.

Key milestones: 1960s — movement born; 1970s — consumer groups write articles, hold exhibitions; 1985 — UN Guidelines for Consumer Protection; 1986 — India enacts COPRA; 2019 — COPRA amended (online buying included, manufacturer also held responsible).

Consumers International: umbrella body with 200+ member organisations from 100+ countries. Foundation for global consumer movement.

India observes 24 December as National Consumers' Day — the day COPRA was enacted in 1986.

Let's Work These Out — Consumer in the Marketplace (pg 77)
1
What are the various ways by which people may be exploited in the market?
Answer
  • Underweighting/Undermeasuring: Shopkeepers weigh less than they should, or measure shorter lengths.
  • Adulteration: Mixing inferior or harmful substances in food items like milk, edible oil, spices, or medicines.
  • Selling defective goods: Knowingly selling products with manufacturing defects or substandard quality.
  • Hidden charges: Traders add charges that were not mentioned before purchase.
  • Overcharging: Charging more than the Maximum Retail Price (MRP) printed on the pack.
  • False advertising: Passing false information through media — e.g., claiming baby powder milk is better than mother's milk.
  • Suppressing safety information: Not disclosing health hazards — cigarette companies hiding cancer links for decades.
  • Hoarding and black marketing: Artificially creating shortages to raise prices.
  • Forced bundling: Making consumers buy unwanted products (e.g., buy toothpaste only if you also buy a toothbrush).
  • Medical negligence: Hospitals providing substandard care that harms patients (Reji Mathew case).
3
What do you think should be the role of government to protect consumers?
Answer
  • Enact and enforce laws: Laws like COPRA give consumers legal rights and an accessible redressal system.
  • Set up quality standards: ISI, FSSAI, Agmark, Hallmark certifications ensure minimum safety and quality standards.
  • Price regulation: Mandate MRP on all packaged goods.
  • Spread consumer awareness: "Jago Grahak Jago" campaigns; RTI Act for government accountability.
  • Create redressal machinery: District, State, and National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commissions.
  • Regulate advertising: Prevent false and misleading advertisements.
  • Fund consumer organisations: Government grants to voluntary consumer groups for awareness work.
Let's Work These Out — Consumer Movement (pg 78)
1
What could have been the steps taken by consumer groups?
Answer
  • Writing articles in newspapers to spread consumer awareness about exploitation and rights.
  • Holding public exhibitions to educate people about market malpractices.
  • Forming consumer groups to investigate malpractices in ration shops and public transport.
  • Organising campaigns and public meetings to demand government action.
  • Filing complaints and court cases against companies with unfair practices.
  • Lobbying government to enact consumer protection laws — resulting in COPRA 1986.
  • Pressuring companies to accept false claims — forcing baby formula companies to admit misleading advertising.
  • Helping individual consumers file cases in consumer commissions.
2
There may be rules and regulations but they are often not followed. Why?
Answer
  • Weak enforcement: Not enough inspectors to monitor millions of sellers and producers.
  • Corruption: Enforcement officers may be bribed to overlook violations.
  • Consumer unawareness: Most consumers don't know their rights and don't complain — allowing violations to continue.
  • Lengthy legal process: Redressal is cumbersome, expensive, and time-consuming — discouraging complaints.
  • Lack of cash memos: In most purchases cash memos are not issued, making evidence difficult to gather.
  • Small transaction size: For small purchases, consumers feel it's not worth the effort to pursue complaints.
  • Business influence: Large companies with political connections may delay enforcement against them.

1
Price sometimes higher or lower than MRP. Discuss possible reasons. Should consumer groups do something?
Answer

Lower than MRP: Competition between shops; bulk-buying discounts; products nearing expiry; consumer bargains (legal — MRP is maximum, not mandatory).

Higher than MRP: Product shortage; remote location with no competition; seller exploits consumer ignorance (illegal — charging above MRP violates consumer rights).

Should consumer groups act? Yes — they should create awareness about MRP rights, help file complaints against overcharging, report violations to commissions, and conduct market surveys. Consumers can also bargain to pay less than MRP and should always check it before paying.

3
RTI Act gives power to question lack of civic amenities. Do you agree?
Answer

Yes. The RTI Act (October 2005) ensures citizens can access information about all government departments' functions. This means:

  • Citizens can ask why a road has not been repaired despite budget allocation.
  • They can demand information about tenders and spending for water/sanitation projects.
  • Like Amritha's case — she used RTI to get her delayed job result from a government department.
  • Government officials must respond within 30 days, creating accountability. Consumer groups have used RTI to expose corruption in public distribution systems.

5
If standardisation ensures quality, why are many goods available without ISI or Agmark certification?
Answer

Because certification is not compulsory for all products. It is mandatory only for products that affect health and safety — LPG cylinders, food colours and additives, cement, packaged drinking water. For most other goods, certification is voluntary. Many small producers can't afford the process; weak enforcement allows uncertified products to circulate even where mandatory; and consumer unawareness means people don't demand these marks.

NCERT Exercises (pg 87–88)
1
Why are rules and regulations required in the marketplace? Illustrate with a few examples.
Answer

Rules are required because the marketplace is inherently unequal — producers are few and powerful, consumers are many and scattered. Without rules, exploitation is inevitable.

  • Underweighting: Without weights and measures regulations, shopkeepers could cheat with false scales. Rules mandate standard weights and penalties.
  • Adulteration: Without food safety laws, traders could mix harmful substances in food. FSSAI and Agmark certifications ensure quality.
  • False advertising: A baby formula company claimed its product was better than mother's milk. Rules now mandate truthful advertising.
  • Cigarettes and cancer: Companies hid the cancer link for decades. Rules now require health warnings on all tobacco products.
  • Overcharging: Without price labelling rules, sellers could charge any price. Mandatory MRP protects consumers.
2
What factors gave birth to the consumer movement in India? Trace its evolution.
Answer

Factors: Rampant food shortages, hoarding, black marketing, widespread adulteration of food and edible oil in the 1960s; absence of any legal system to protect consumers; the prevailing attitude that consumers must be careful themselves — sellers had no accountability after a sale.

3
Explain the need for consumer consciousness by giving two examples.
Answer

Example 1 — Reji Mathew (Medical Negligence): Reji was permanently crippled due to improper anaesthesia during a routine tonsil operation. His father was conscious of Reji's rights and pursued the case to the National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission, winning compensation after the State Commission dismissed it. Without consumer consciousness and persistence, the hospital would never have been held accountable.

Example 2 — Abirami (Right to Choose): Abirami was denied a refund by a coaching institute when she wanted to leave midway. Because she was conscious of her right to choose, she filed in the District Commission. She won her refund, the institute was fined Rs 25,000, and — most importantly — the State Commission banned all educational institutions in the state from collecting full-course fees in advance. One conscious consumer's action protected thousands of future students.

4
Mention a few factors which cause exploitation of consumers.
Answer
  • Unequal power: Producers few and powerful; consumers many and scattered.
  • Consumer unawareness: Most consumers don't know their rights — don't check MRP, expiry dates, or insist on bills.
  • Limited choices: In many areas only one supplier — no competition means no incentive to treat consumers fairly.
  • False advertising: Large companies with massive media reach can mislead consumers with exaggerated claims.
  • Weak enforcement: Rules exist but enforcement is weak — officials may be corrupt or understaffed.
  • Complex/expensive redressal: Filing complaints requires time, money, documentation, often a lawyer.
  • No cash memos: Most small retail purchases don't have bills, making it hard to prove exploitation.
5
What is the rationale behind the enactment of Consumer Protection Act 1986?
Answer
  • Fill a legal vacuum: No dedicated legal system to protect consumers from exploitation before COPRA.
  • Balance power: Give weak individual consumers legal tools to stand up against powerful producers.
  • Establish 6 consumer rights: Safety, information, choice, redressal, representation, consumer education.
  • Accessible justice: Create an affordable three-tier quasi-judicial system — separate from the slow, expensive regular courts.
  • Shift responsibility: Move burden of product quality from consumer to producer/seller.
  • Support consumer movement: Give legal backing to years of struggle by consumer groups.
6
Describe some of your duties as consumers if you visit a shopping complex.
Answer
  • Check MRP before paying — never pay more than the printed price.
  • Check expiry dates on food, medicines, and beauty products.
  • Insist on a bill/receipt for every purchase — proof of purchase for future complaints.
  • Look for ISI, Agmark, or Hallmark depending on the product (electronics, food, jewellery).
  • Read labels — check ingredients, weight, and information on packaged goods.
  • Don't be swayed by misleading offers — evaluate "scratch and win" and "free gift" schemes carefully.
  • Report unfair practices to consumer groups or commissions.
  • Demand correct weight — ensure shopkeeper weighs accurately.
7
You buy a bottle of honey and a biscuit packet. Which logo or mark will you look for and why?
Answer

For honey — Agmark: Agmark (Agricultural Mark) certifies purity and quality standards for agricultural products including honey, edible oils, cereals, and spices. It assures that the honey has not been adulterated with sugar syrup or artificial substances.

For biscuit packet — FSSAI logo: The FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority of India) logo certifies food safety standards. The FSSAI licence number on the pack assures the product meets food safety regulations — no harmful additives, proper hygiene, correct labelling.

8
What legal measures were taken by the government to empower consumers in India?
Answer
  • COPRA 1986: Landmark legislation establishing 6 consumer rights and three-tier quasi-judicial redressal system.
  • COPRA Amendment 2019: Extended to online purchases; manufacturer also responsible; mediation; penalties including imprisonment.
  • RTI Act 2005: Citizens can access information about government departments — accountability for public services.
  • Mandatory quality certifications: ISI/BIS for industrial products, Agmark for agricultural products, Hallmark for jewellery, FSSAI for food.
  • Weights and Measures Act: Standard weights, measures, and mandatory MRP labelling on packaged goods.
  • Consumer Affairs Departments: COPRA led to separate departments in central and state governments for consumer awareness and law implementation.
  • Financial support: Government funds voluntary consumer groups to create awareness.

10
By what means can the consumers express their solidarity?
Answer
  • Join consumer groups: Local consumer forums, Residents' Welfare Associations — collective voice is stronger.
  • Class action suits: Filing group complaints in Consumer Commissions — amplifies individual complaints into impactful collective action.
  • Boycotts: Collectively refusing to buy from companies with unfair practices — economic pressure changes behaviour.
  • Campaigns: Participating in consumer awareness campaigns. People's campaigns have already influenced WTO decisions globally.
  • Spreading awareness: Sharing information about consumer rights with family, friends, and community.
  • Social media: Posting about marketplace exploitation creates pressure on companies and spreads awareness rapidly.
  • Demanding bills: Collectively insisting on cash memos creates accountability and reduces tax evasion.
11
Critically examine the progress of consumer movement in India.
Answer

Progress made: COPRA 1986 — landmark law; India has exclusive consumer redressal authorities (rare globally); 2000+ consumer groups; RTI Act 2005; COPRA 2019 amendment; 24 December National Consumers' Day; Consumers International membership.

Challenges remaining: Only 50-60 of 2000+ groups are well organised; redressal process is cumbersome, expensive, and time-consuming; most purchases don't have cash memos; consumer awareness spreading but slowly; enforcement of laws is weak; unorganised sector workers still unprotected.

Conclusion: The consumer movement has made meaningful institutional progress but remains far from its potential. Active consumer participation — from every individual — is essential for the movement to be truly effective.

Important Terms to Know

COPRA

Consumer Protection Act 1986 (amended 2019). Landmark Indian law establishing 6 consumer rights and a three-tier quasi-judicial redressal system. Enacted 24 December.

Consumer Movement

Social force born in India in the 1960s due to food shortages, hoarding, and adulteration. Arose from dissatisfaction with unfair practices and absence of legal protection.

Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission

Three-tier quasi-judicial machinery under COPRA (District/State/National) for resolving consumer complaints and awarding compensation.

RTI Act

Right to Information Act (October 2005). Citizens can access information about all government department functions. Extended consumer rights to public services.

Consumers International

Global umbrella body — 200+ member organisations from 100+ countries. Foundation of global consumer movement, built on UN Guidelines (1985).

MRP

Maximum Retail Price — the maximum price printed on the package. Sellers cannot charge more. Consumers can bargain to pay less.

ISI Mark

Indian Standards Institute mark (now BIS). Certifies industrial products meet quality standards. Mandatory for safety-critical products like LPG cylinders.

Agmark

Agricultural Mark — certification for agricultural products like edible oil, honey, cereals, and spices. Ensures quality and purity standards.

Hallmark

BIS certification for gold and silver jewellery purity. Mandatory for gold jewellery above a threshold weight.

Unfair Trade Practices

Deceptive practices exploiting consumers — underweighting, adulteration, selling defective goods, false advertising, hiding health hazards, overcharging above MRP.

National Consumers' Day

24 December — the day COPRA was enacted in 1986. Observed annually to promote consumer awareness and rights.

Consumer Forum

Voluntary organisations that guide consumers, represent them in commissions, and receive government funds for awareness work.

Important Facts for Exams
  • COPRA = Consumer Protection Act 1986, amended in 2019. Enacted on 24 December — now National Consumers' Day.
  • Consumer movement in India began in an organised form in the 1960s due to food shortages, hoarding, black marketing, and adulteration.
  • India is one of the few countries in the world that have exclusive authorities for consumer disputes redressal.
  • Three-tier system: District (up to Rs 1 crore) → State (Rs 1–10 crore) → National (above Rs 10 crore).
  • Consumers International: 200+ member organisations from 100+ countries. Based on UN Guidelines (1985).
  • India has 2000+ consumer groups; only 50–60 are well organised and recognised.
  • ISI = manufactured goods (BIS). Agmark = agricultural products. Hallmark = gold/silver jewellery.
  • Certification is mandatory only for safety-critical products — LPG cylinders, food colours, cement, packaged drinking water. Voluntary for others.
  • RTI Act was enacted in October 2005 — right to information about government department functions.
  • 6 Consumer Rights: Safety, Information, Choice, Redressal, Representation, Consumer Education.
  • COPRA 2019: Online purchases covered; manufacturer also responsible; mediation introduced; imprisonment possible for violations.
  • Consumer can file a complaint without a lawyer, physically or online, and conduct case via video conferencing.
