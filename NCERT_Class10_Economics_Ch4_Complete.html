MNCs and Production Across Countries
How multinationals spread production globally

A Multinational Corporation (MNC) is a company that owns or controls production in more than one nation. MNCs set up factories and offices in regions where they can get cheap labour and other resources to keep costs low and earn greater profits.

Example — Industrial Equipment MNC:
Design → USA | Components → China | Assembly → Mexico & Eastern Europe | Customer care → India (Bengaluru call centres) | Products sold → Worldwide

Result: 50-60% cost savings!

4 ways MNCs operate in other countries:

  • Set up factories/offices directly (Ford Motors — ₹1700 crore plant near Chennai)
  • Set up production jointly with local companies (Ford + Mahindra & Mahindra)
  • Buy up local companies (Cargill Foods bought Parakh Foods — now largest edible oil producer in India)
  • Place orders with small local producers (garments, footwear, sports items)

The money spent by MNCs to buy assets (land, buildings, machines) is called foreign investment.

What is Globalisation?
Integration of production and markets

Globalisation is the process of rapid integration or interconnection between countries through greater foreign trade, foreign investment, and the movement of goods, services, investments, and technology across national borders.

Foreign trade integrates markets: When Chinese toys enter India, Indian buyers get more choice at lower prices. Prices of similar goods in the two markets tend to become equal. Producers in the two countries compete even though separated by thousands of miles.

3 factors enabling globalisation:
1. Technology — improved transport (containers), telecommunications, IT, internet
2. Liberalisation — India removed trade barriers from 1991
3. WTO pressure — forced developing countries to open up trade
WTO and Trade Liberalisation
The unfair bargain between developed and developing countries

The World Trade Organisation (WTO) was started at the initiative of developed countries. Its aim is to liberalise international trade. About 160 countries are WTO members.

WTO establishes rules for international trade and sees that these rules are obeyed. In theory, free trade for all. In practice:

The unfair reality: WTO rules have forced developing countries to remove trade barriers. But developed countries have unfairly retained their barriers and still pay their farmers huge subsidies.

Example: US agriculture = 1% of GDP, 0.5% of employment — yet US farmers get massive government subsidies, sell farm produce at abnormally low prices globally, hurting Indian farmers.

India had trade barriers after Independence (1950s-60s) to protect young domestic industries. In 1991, India liberalised — removing many of these barriers as part of economic reforms supported by WTO.

Impact of Globalisation — Winners and Losers
Benefits are not shared equally

Who benefited: Well-off consumers (more choice, lower prices); top Indian companies (improved technology, new markets); Indian companies that became MNCs (Tata Motors, Infosys, Ranbaxy); IT and service sector workers.

Who was hurt: Small producers (Ravi's capacitor unit — output halved, workers reduced from 20 to 7 after 2001 import liberalisation); workers (Sushila — went from permanent worker to temporary, lost benefits, works 7 days 7:30am-10pm).

Key industries where small manufacturers were hit: batteries, capacitors, plastics, toys, tyres, dairy products, vegetable oil. Small and medium industries employ 11 crore workers — the second largest employer after agriculture.

Fair globalisation = creating opportunities for all + ensuring benefits are shared better. Government must protect interests of all, not just the rich. People's organisations have already influenced WTO decisions through campaigns.

Complete the statement showing how production in the garment industry is spread across countries (starting with cotton fibre from Korea...).

The brand tag says 'Made in Thailand' but they are not Thai products. We dissect the manufacturing process and look for the best solution at each step. We are doing it globally. In making garments, the company may, for example, get:

Let's Work These Out — Ford Motors / MNCs (pg 59)
1
Would you say Ford Motors is a MNC? Why?
Answer

Yes, Ford Motors is definitely a MNC. A multinational corporation is a company that owns or controls production in more than one nation.

Ford Motors qualifies because: it is an American company with production spread over 26 countries of the world; it set up a large plant near Chennai, India (in collaboration with Mahindra and Mahindra); it both sells in Indian markets and exports to South Africa, Mexico, Brazil, and the USA from its Indian plant. This global production and sales network across many nations makes it a classic MNC.

2
What is foreign investment? How much did Ford Motors invest in India?
Answer

Foreign investment is the money spent by a MNC to buy assets (land, buildings, machines, equipment) in another country. Any investment is made with the hope that these assets will earn profits.

Ford Motors invested ₹1700 crore to set up a large production plant near Chennai, India. This was done in collaboration with Mahindra and Mahindra, an Indian company.

3
MNCs tap not only the large markets that countries like India provide, but also lower costs of production. Explain.
Answer

Large markets: India has over 1.4 billion people, with a growing middle class that is increasingly purchasing cars. By 2017, Ford was selling 88,000 cars in India — a vast and growing consumer base that justifies the investment.

Lower costs of production:

  • Cheap labour: Indian workers earn far less than American or European workers — significantly reducing the wage bill for car manufacturing.
  • Local components: Many local Indian companies supply auto parts and components at competitive rates.
  • Skilled engineers: India has skilled engineers who understand technical aspects of car production.
  • English-speaking workforce: India's large English-speaking educated youth provides cheap customer care and technical support.

The combination of a huge market + low production costs makes India extremely attractive — Ford not only sells in India but also exports 1,81,000 cars to South Africa, Mexico, Brazil, and USA — using India as a global production base.

4
Why does Ford want to develop India as a base for manufacturing car components for its global operations? Discuss: (a) cost of labour, (b) local auto-parts manufacturers, (c) closeness to buyers in India and China.
Answer

(a) Cost of labour and other resources in India: Indian labour is significantly cheaper than labour in the US or Europe. Other resources like electricity, land, and materials are also cheaper. This makes producing components in India more cost-effective, improving global profit margins.

(b) Presence of local manufacturers who supply auto parts: India has a well-developed auto components industry. Several Indian companies supply parts like gears, transmissions, electrical systems, etc., at competitive prices. This availability of a local supply chain reduces logistics costs and ensures quality at lower prices.

(c) Closeness to a large number of buyers in India and China: India and China together have billions of consumers with rapidly growing car ownership. Manufacturing components in India positions Ford close to the world's two largest and fastest-growing automobile markets, reducing export costs and enabling faster delivery to nearby markets.

5
In what ways will production of cars by Ford Motors in India lead to interlinking of production?
Answer
  • Ford's Indian plant uses components from Indian auto parts suppliers — linking Indian secondary sector to Ford's global production chain.
  • Cars assembled in India are exported to South Africa, Mexico, Brazil, and USA — linking Indian production to global consumption markets.
  • Car components manufactured in India are exported to Ford's factories in other countries for assembly — linking Indian component production to global assembly lines.
  • Ford's design and engineering work is done in the US, components from multiple countries, assembly in India, and sales globally — one product involving production across many nations.
  • Indian service sector companies (IT, banking, logistics) that support Ford's operations become part of this global interlinking.

7
Nearly all major multinationals are American, Japanese or European (Nike, Coca-Cola, Honda, Nokia). Can you guess why?
Answer
  • Early industrialisation: The US, Europe, and Japan industrialised much earlier (18th-19th centuries). They developed manufacturing expertise, capital, and technology long before developing countries.
  • Vast capital accumulated: Decades of industrialisation gave these companies enormous financial resources to expand globally.
  • Advanced technology: They developed breakthrough technologies (automobiles, electronics, soft drinks, IT) that created globally competitive products.
  • Colonial advantage: European countries controlled many colonies — raw materials, markets, and trade networks were already established globally.
  • Strong home markets: Large, wealthy domestic consumer markets in the US, Europe, and Japan gave these companies the scale needed to first become strong domestically, then expand globally.
  • Strong IP protection: Patent laws and brand protection allowed these companies to build powerful global brands.
Let's Work These Out — Foreign Trade & Integration (pg 61)
1
What was the main channel connecting countries in the past? How is it different now?
Answer

In the past: Foreign trade was the main channel connecting countries. Raw materials, food stuff, and finished products moved across borders. Colonies like India exported raw materials and imported finished goods. Trade routes (East-West via India) were the main links.

Today it is different because in addition to trade in goods, we now have:

  • Foreign direct investment by MNCs — companies set up actual production facilities across countries
  • Movement of services — IT, banking, call centres, design work crossing borders
  • Movement of technology — know-how, patents, software moving globally
  • Movement of investments — financial capital flowing between countries

Today, not just final goods but production itself is spread across countries. MNCs have added production integration on top of the older trade linkages.

3
China imports steel from India. Explain how this affects: (a) steel companies in China, (b) steel companies in India, (c) industries buying steel in China.
Answer

(a) Steel companies in China: They face increased competition from cheaper Indian steel imports. If Indian steel is cheaper, Chinese steel companies will face reduced sales, lower profits, and may have to cut prices to compete. Some might even shut down if they cannot compete — their workers could lose jobs.

(b) Steel companies in India: They benefit greatly — they now have access to the huge Chinese market in addition to the domestic market. Greater demand means increased production, more employment, higher revenues, and profits for Indian steel companies. It provides an opportunity to expand production.

(c) Industries buying steel in China: They benefit significantly. With Indian steel imports competing with Chinese steel, prices of steel in China will fall (or at least not rise as fast). Lower steel prices reduce their production costs, making their products cheaper and more competitive globally.

4
How will import of steel from India into China lead to integration of markets for steel in the two countries?
Answer

When India exports steel to China, trade connects the two previously separate steel markets:

  • Indian steel producers can now sell in both Indian and Chinese markets — expanding their reach.
  • Chinese steel buyers now have a choice between Chinese and Indian steel — increasing competition.
  • If Indian steel is cheaper, prices of steel in China will fall towards Indian price levels — the two markets are now linked by price.
  • Any change in Indian steel prices will now affect Chinese prices too — producers in both countries compete with each other despite being thousands of miles apart.

This is exactly what the NCERT chapter means by "integration of markets" — trade causes markets in different countries to become connected, with prices, competition, and supply-demand conditions influencing each other across borders.

Let's Work These Out — What is Globalisation (pg 62)
1
What is the role of MNCs in the globalisation process?
Answer

MNCs are the key drivers of globalisation:

  • They spread production across multiple countries, creating global production networks.
  • They make foreign direct investments — linking countries through capital flows.
  • They control large parts of foreign trade — Ford's Indian plant exports to multiple countries.
  • They transfer technology between countries, enabling developing nations to access advanced production methods.
  • They create global supply chains — the production of one product involves many countries.
  • They integrate markets — their global operations connect consumers and producers across countries.

The chapter says: "MNCs are playing a major role in the globalisation process. More and more goods and services, investments and technology are moving between countries" because of MNC activities.

2
What are the various ways in which countries can be linked?
Answer
  • Foreign trade: Import and export of goods and services — the oldest and most important link.
  • Foreign investment: MNCs setting up production facilities in other countries.
  • Technology transfer: New production methods, software, patents moving between countries.
  • Movement of services: IT, banking, education, healthcare, call centres crossing borders.
  • Movement of people: People migrating for better income, jobs, or education — though this has been restricted in recent decades.
  • Cultural and information links: Internet, satellite TV, social media connecting people globally (though this chapter focuses on economic links).
3
Globalisation, by connecting countries, shall result in: (a) lesser competition, (b) greater competition, (c) no change in competition.
Correct answer: (b) Greater competition among producers

When countries are connected through trade and investment, producers in each country now compete not just with domestic producers but also with foreign producers. A Chinese toy maker competes with an Indian toy maker even though they are in different countries. An Indian steel company competes with Chinese steel companies. This expanded competition — both local and foreign producers competing — is a key feature of globalisation.

Let's Work These Out — IT in Globalisation (pg 63)
2
How is information technology connected with globalisation? Would globalisation have been possible without IT?
Answer

IT is deeply connected with globalisation:

  • Internet enables instant communication across the world — the London office can send text and design instructions to the Delhi printing office instantly.
  • E-banking allows instant payment across international borders — a London bank to a Delhi bank in seconds.
  • Telecommunications allow MNCs to manage factories and offices in dozens of countries from headquarters in one location.
  • IT has enabled the outsourcing of services — call centres in Bengaluru serving US customers, data entry for European companies done in India.
  • Video conferencing reduces the need for international travel while maintaining global business coordination.

Would globalisation have been possible without IT? No — not in its current form. While trade between countries existed for centuries without IT, the complex global production networks, real-time coordination of factories across continents, and the massive expansion of service exports from countries like India would have been impossible without modern IT. IT has dramatically reduced the cost of communication across distances, making global production coordination practical and affordable.

Let's Work These Out — Liberalisation (pg 64)
1
What do you understand by liberalisation of foreign trade?
Answer

Liberalisation means removing barriers or restrictions set by the government on foreign trade and foreign investment.

Before liberalisation (pre-1991), India had trade barriers — taxes on imports, quotas, restrictions on foreign investment — to protect domestic industries from foreign competition. Starting around 1991, India removed many of these barriers.

With liberalisation of trade:

  • Businesses can make decisions freely about what to import or export without needing government approval for everything.
  • Goods can be imported and exported more easily.
  • Foreign companies can set up factories and offices in India.
  • The government imposes far fewer restrictions than before — it is said to be "more liberal."

The rationale was that competition from foreign goods and companies would push Indian producers to improve quality and efficiency.

2
How can quotas be used as trade barriers? Using the example of Chinese toys, can this be used?
Answer

Quotas are limits placed on the quantity of goods that can be imported into a country in a given period.

Using Chinese toys example: If the Indian government imposes a quota — say, only 1 crore Chinese toys can be imported per year — then the total supply of Chinese toys in India would be limited. With limited supply, prices of Chinese toys would rise (or at least not fall as much), and Indian toy makers would face less severe competition and could continue selling.

Should quotas be used? There are two perspectives:

  • For quotas: They protect Indian toy manufacturers and their workers from being wiped out by cheap imports. They protect livelihoods and allow time for Indian industry to improve competitiveness.
  • Against quotas: They raise prices for Indian consumers who benefit from cheaper, more varied Chinese toys. They reduce competition which might make Indian toy makers complacent about improving quality.

The best policy depends on the situation — infant industries may need short-term protection, but indefinite protection can harm consumers and reduce efficiency.

2
What do you think can be done so that trade between countries is more fair?
Answer
  • Equal rules for all: Developed countries should also remove their trade barriers and farm subsidies — not just force developing countries to do so.
  • Developing country alliance: India and other developing countries should collectively negotiate at WTO to fight unfair practices of developed countries.
  • Allow developing countries protection: Just as today's developed countries protected their industries during early development, developing countries should be allowed to protect vulnerable sectors like agriculture.
  • Remove agricultural subsidies: Developed countries (especially the US and EU) should stop giving huge subsidies to their farmers that enable unfair competition in global markets.
  • Compensate affected countries: When developed country farm exports damage farmers in developing countries, there should be compensation mechanisms.
  • People's campaigns: Civil society organisations and people's campaigns can pressure WTO and governments to make fairer rules — as already demonstrated in WTO meetings.
Let's Work These Out — Impact of Globalisation (pg 67)
1
How has competition benefited people in India?
Answer
  • More choice: Indian consumers can now choose from a wide range of products — cars, mobile phones, electronics, clothes — from world-class manufacturers. Gone are the days when only Ambassador and Fiat cars were available.
  • Lower prices: Competition forces companies to be efficient and reduce costs, benefiting consumers with lower prices.
  • Better quality: To compete, both Indian and foreign companies have had to improve product quality.
  • Indian companies improved: Top Indian companies invested in newer technology and production methods, raised standards, and some have emerged as MNCs themselves (Tata Motors, Infosys, Ranbaxy).
  • New jobs created: In industries like IT, automobiles, electronics, and services, new employment opportunities emerged.
3
Why do governments try to attract more foreign investment?
Answer
  • Employment creation: MNCs set up factories and offices that create jobs — directly in their plants and indirectly in supplier companies.
  • Technology transfer: MNCs bring latest production technology and management practices that improve productivity across the economy.
  • Export earnings: MNCs often export from their Indian facilities — earning foreign exchange for the country (Ford exported 1,81,000 cars from India).
  • Tax revenue: Foreign companies pay taxes, increasing government revenue for public services.
  • Infrastructure development: To attract investment, governments build better infrastructure (roads, power, SEZs) that also benefits domestic industry.
  • Upgrading local industry: Local companies supplying parts and services to MNCs improve their quality and efficiency.
Let's Work These Out — Small Producers & Workers (pg 68 & 70)
1
What are the ways in which Ravi's small production unit was affected by rising competition?
Answer

Ravi started a capacitor manufacturing unit in Hosur (Tamil Nadu) in 1992 and had 20 workers within 3 years. After 2001 when import restrictions on capacitors were removed (WTO agreement):

  • His main customers — Indian television companies — were forced by competition from MNC TV brands to shift to assembly work for MNCs.
  • When these TV companies bought capacitors, they preferred cheaper imported ones (half the price of Ravi's) over Ravi's locally-made ones.
  • Ravi's production fell to less than half of what he made in 2000.
  • His workforce shrank from 20 workers to just 7.
  • Many of his fellow small manufacturers in Hyderabad and Chennai shut down completely.

This is the "compete or perish" reality of globalisation for small producers who cannot match the economies of scale and pricing of imported goods backed by large MNC supply chains.

1
In what ways has competition affected workers, Indian exporters and foreign MNCs in the garment industry?
Answer

Workers (like Sushila):

  • Permanent jobs replaced by temporary/flexible employment — no job security
  • Loss of benefits: no PF, no health insurance, no overtime at double rate
  • Very long working hours — 7:30am to 10pm, 7 days a week
  • Lower wages — Sushila earns less than half her earlier salary
  • Workers are denied fair share of globalisation's benefits

Indian garment exporters:

  • Face intense competition from exporters in other low-cost countries (Bangladesh, Vietnam)
  • Under constant pressure from MNCs to cut prices
  • To survive, they cut labour costs — replacing permanent workers with temporary workers
  • Profits are squeezed; survival requires continuous cost reduction

Foreign MNCs:

  • Benefit greatly — they get cheaply produced quality garments from India
  • Competition among Indian exporters drives prices lower, increasing MNC profits
  • They can dictate terms — price, quality, delivery, and even labour conditions
  • They sell these garments in developed countries at high prices, making large profits
NCERT Exercises (pg 72–73)
1
What do you understand by globalisation? Explain in your own words.
Answer

Globalisation is the process of rapid integration or interconnection between countries through greater foreign trade, foreign investment by MNCs, and the movement of goods, services, technology, and investments across national borders.

In simple terms: earlier, each country produced most things it needed domestically. Now, a single product (like a car or a T-shirt) may have components designed in one country, manufactured in several others, assembled in another, and sold globally. Countries, markets, and production systems are deeply interconnected.

As a result of globalisation: Indian consumers can buy phones made by Korean or American companies; Indian IT companies serve clients in Europe; an Indian worker's job may depend on orders from a UK retailer. The world has become one large, interconnected market where national boundaries matter much less for economic activity than before.

2
What were the reasons for putting barriers to foreign trade and investment by the Indian government? Why did it wish to remove these barriers?
Answer

Reasons for barriers (pre-1991):

  • Protect infant industries: Industries in 1950s-60s India were just coming up. Foreign competition at that stage would have destroyed them before they could establish themselves.
  • Self-reliance: India wanted to develop its own industrial capacity rather than depend on foreign goods.
  • Conserve foreign exchange: India had limited foreign exchange and could not afford to spend it on imports of consumer goods.
  • All developed countries did this: The US, Europe, and Japan all protected their domestic industries during their early development phase.

Reasons for removing barriers (1991 onwards):

  • Indian industries had matured: By 1991, it was felt that Indian producers were ready to compete globally.
  • Competition improves quality: Exposing Indian producers to foreign competition would force them to improve quality and efficiency.
  • International pressure: Powerful international organisations (WTO, IMF, World Bank) pushed India to liberalise.
  • Attract foreign investment: Removing barriers would attract MNCs to invest in India — bringing capital, technology, and jobs.
3
How would flexibility in labour laws help companies?
Answer

Flexibility in labour laws helps companies primarily by reducing labour costs:

  • Hire and fire freely: Companies can employ workers during peak periods and dismiss them when orders fall — they don't pay workers throughout the year.
  • Avoid permanent worker obligations: Permanent workers are entitled to PF, gratuity, paid leave, medical benefits. Temporary workers get none of these — saving the company significant costs.
  • Flexible working hours: Companies can make workers work extra hours during peak season without mandatory overtime payments.
  • Lower overall wage bill: Temporary workers often earn less than permanent workers for the same work.

While this helps companies compete and attract foreign investment, it harms workers — as Sushila's case shows: from a permanent worker with full benefits to a temporary worker earning less than half her earlier salary.

4
What are the various ways in which MNCs set up, control or produce in other countries?
Answer

MNCs use four main ways to operate in other countries:

  • Set up new factories/offices directly: Ford Motors set up a ₹1700 crore plant near Chennai. The MNC invests its own money to build from scratch in the host country.
  • Set up joint ventures with local companies: Ford partnered with Mahindra and Mahindra. The local company provides knowledge of the local market, while the MNC brings capital and technology — both benefit.
  • Buy up (acquire) existing local companies: Cargill Foods bought Parakh Foods — taking over their marketing network, 4 oil refineries, and brand reputation. This is the most common route for MNC expansion.
  • Place orders with small local producers: MNCs in garments, footwear, sports items order from small Indian producers. The MNC then sells under its own brand. The MNC determines price, quality, delivery, and even labour conditions for these small producers.
5
Why do developed countries want developing countries to liberalise their trade and investment? What should the developing countries demand in return?
Answer

Why developed countries want developing countries to liberalise:

  • Developing countries have large populations = huge potential markets for developed country products (cars, electronics, food, medicines)
  • Developing countries have cheap labour and resources where MNCs can profitably set up production
  • Removing developing country trade barriers allows MNCs to sell their products in these markets without competition from protected local industries

What developing countries should demand in return:

  • Developed countries must also remove their trade barriers — equal rules for all
  • Developed countries must stop farm subsidies that let them sell agricultural products at artificially low prices, destroying developing country farmers
  • Developing countries should be allowed to protect key sectors (like agriculture, which employs most of their workers) even as they open others
  • Fair technology transfer — not just access to products but actual transfer of technology
  • Greater representation and voting power in WTO decision-making
6
"The impact of globalisation has not been uniform." Explain this statement.
Answer

The impact of globalisation has been very different for different groups of people:

Those who benefited:

  • Well-off consumers — more choice, better quality, lower prices
  • Top Indian companies — improved technology, grew into MNCs (Tata, Infosys)
  • IT and service workers — new well-paying jobs in IT, call centres, banking
  • Local suppliers to MNCs — prospered from new business

Those who were hurt:

  • Small producers like Ravi — lost clients, production halved, workers let go
  • Workers like Sushila — lost permanent jobs, forced into temporary work with lower pay and no benefits
  • Small manufacturers in batteries, capacitors, toys, plastics — many shut down
  • Farmers — faced competition from subsidised imports from developed countries

The pattern: people with education, skill, and wealth made the best use of new opportunities. Small producers and workers often faced severe disruption. Hence, globalisation has been uneven in its benefits.

7
How has liberalisation of trade and investment policies helped the globalisation process?
Answer

Liberalisation has been a key enabler of globalisation:

  • Goods can move freely: Without import taxes and quotas, goods produced cheaply in one country can be sold in another — as Chinese toys entered India after liberalisation.
  • Foreign companies can invest: When foreign investment restrictions were removed, MNCs could set up factories in India — Ford, Samsung, etc. invested billions.
  • Competition increases: With fewer restrictions, Indian producers face global competition — forcing efficiency improvements.
  • Services can be exported: Liberalisation of service sectors allowed Indian IT companies, call centres, and banks to serve global clients.
  • Markets integrate: As barriers fall, prices in different countries converge and markets become interconnected — the defining feature of globalisation.

Without liberalisation, even if technology improved, goods and investments could not have moved freely — limiting integration. Liberalisation created the legal and policy environment that made globalisation possible.

8
How does foreign trade lead to integration of markets across countries? Explain with an example other than those given here.
Answer

Foreign trade leads to market integration because when goods travel from one market to another, competition and price signals connect them.

Example — Mobile Phones:

  • Korean companies (Samsung) and Chinese companies (Xiaomi) export mobile phones to India.
  • Indian buyers now choose between Indian and imported brands — more competition, more choice.
  • Prices of phones in India are influenced by global manufacturing costs in China and Korea.
  • When Chinese manufacturing costs fall, phone prices globally (including India) tend to fall.
  • Indian phone makers must match international price and quality standards — they cannot charge much more than imported equivalents.
  • Thus the Indian phone market and the global phone market are now tightly integrated — they influence each other through trade.
9
Globalisation will continue in the future. Can you imagine what the world would be like twenty years from now? Give reasons.
Answer

Twenty years from now, globalisation will likely be even deeper, with some new features:

  • Digital globalisation: AI, automation, and digital services will make it even cheaper to provide services across borders — more outsourcing, more remote work.
  • More countries as MNC homes: Indian companies (Tata, Infosys), Chinese companies (Alibaba, Huawei), and others from developing countries will become major global MNCs alongside the current American/European/Japanese ones.
  • Green globalisation: Climate agreements will integrate environmental standards globally — countries will have to meet common carbon reduction targets.
  • Greater movement of people: Labour migration between countries may increase as demographic imbalances grow (ageing populations in developed countries, young populations in developing ones).
  • Counter-globalisation forces: National protectionism may also rise — some countries may impose new barriers due to concerns about job losses and national security (as seen with US-China tensions).
10
Two people argue: One says globalisation has hurt India's development. The other says globalisation is helping India develop. How would you respond?
Answer

Both are partially right — the impact has been uneven, not uniformly positive or negative:

Evidence that globalisation helped:

  • IT sector boomed — millions of high-paying jobs created in Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Chennai
  • Indian companies became global MNCs (Tata Motors, Infosys, Ranbaxy)
  • Consumers enjoy wider choice and better quality at lower prices
  • Foreign investment brought capital, technology, and best practices
  • India's GDP growth accelerated significantly in the post-liberalisation era

Evidence that globalisation hurt:

  • Small manufacturers (Ravi) wiped out by import competition, workers jobless
  • Workers like Sushila lost job security, benefits, and decent wages
  • WTO rules have been unfair — developed countries retained subsidies while forcing India to open up
  • Benefits have been concentrated — the educated, skilled, wealthy benefited most

My response: Globalisation is a reality that cannot be reversed, but it must be made fairer. The government must protect small producers and workers, ensure labour laws are followed, and negotiate better terms at WTO. Globalisation with strong social protection policies can genuinely help India's development.

Important Terms to Know

Multinational Corporation (MNC)

A company that owns or controls production in more than one nation. Sets up factories where labour and resources are cheap to maximise profits.

Foreign Investment

Money spent by MNCs to buy assets (land, buildings, machines, equipment) in another country, with the hope of earning profits.

Globalisation

The process of rapid integration or interconnection between countries through greater foreign trade, foreign investment, and movement of goods, services, technology, and investments.

Integration of Markets

When foreign trade connects markets in different countries — prices equalise, consumers get more choice, and producers compete across borders. Result of globalisation.

Trade Barrier

Restrictions set by governments to regulate foreign trade. Includes taxes (tariffs) on imports and quotas (limits on the quantity of imports). Used to protect domestic producers.

Liberalisation

Removing barriers or restrictions on foreign trade and investment set by the government. India liberalised from around 1991, allowing freer import/export and foreign investment.

World Trade Organisation (WTO)

International organisation (started by developed countries) that aims to liberalise international trade. About 160 member countries. Sets and enforces rules for international trade.

Special Economic Zones (SEZs)

Industrial zones set up by governments to attract foreign investment — with world-class infrastructure, 5-year tax holidays, and flexible labour laws.

Fair Globalisation

A form of globalisation that creates opportunities for all and ensures benefits are shared better — not just by the wealthy and educated, but also by small producers and workers.

Foreign Trade

Import and export of goods and services between countries. Creates opportunities for producers to reach beyond domestic markets and for buyers to access globally produced goods.

Quota

A limit on the quantity of goods that can be imported into a country in a given period. A type of trade barrier used to restrict imports and protect domestic producers.

Flexible Labour

Hiring workers on a temporary/short-term basis instead of permanently, to reduce costs. Workers hired flexibly have no job security, no PF, no paid leave, and lower wages.
