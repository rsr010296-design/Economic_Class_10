What is Development?
Different people, different goals

Development involves thinking about aspirations, desires, and ways to achieve goals — both personal and national. People seek more income, but also equal treatment, freedom, security, and respect.

Key idea: Different persons have different developmental goals. What may be development for one may even be destructive for another.

Example: Industrialists want dams for electricity → but this displaces tribals who prefer small check dams. Same goal (development), conflicting means.

How to Compare Countries
Per capita income & World Bank

Per capita income = Total income of a country ÷ Total population.

World Bank classification (2024):
• High income (rich): ≥ US$ 66,500/year
• Low income: ≤ US$ 2,300/year
• India: ~US$ 11,000 → lower middle income

Limitation: Averages hide inequality. Country A (all earning ~₹10,000) is better than Country B (4 earning ₹500, one earning ₹48,000) even if averages are equal.

Kerala vs Haryana
Income is not everything

Haryana has higher per capita income (₹3,25,759) than Kerala (₹2,81,001), yet:

  • Kerala IMR: 6 per 1000 vs Haryana's 28 — nearly 3× worse
  • Literacy Rate: Kerala 94% vs Haryana 82%
  • Net Attendance (15-17 yrs): Kerala 94% vs Haryana 73%
Reason: Money in your pocket cannot buy all goods & services needed to live well. Public facilities — healthcare, schools, PDS — matter greatly.
Human Development Index (HDI)
UNDP's broader measure

The UNDP's Human Development Report compares countries on: per capita income (PPP $), life expectancy at birth, and mean years of schooling.

India's HDI rank (2023): 130 out of 193 countries. Sri Lanka ranks 89 — far ahead despite being a small neighbour!

Nepal and Bangladesh have lower per capita income than India, yet perform better on life expectancy.

Sustainability of Development
Can today's growth last?

Renewable resources (groundwater, crops) can be replenished but can be overused. 300 Indian districts have seen water levels fall over 4 metres in 20 years.

Non-renewable resources (crude oil, coal) cannot be replenished. World crude oil reserves will last only ~47 years at current rates.

"We have not inherited the world from our forefathers — we have borrowed it from our children."
Let's Work These Out — Section 1 (pg 6)
1
Why do different persons have different notions of development? Which explanation is more important — (a) people are different, or (b) life situations are different?
Answer

Different persons have different notions of development because they have different needs and aspirations shaped by their circumstances.

Option (b) — life situations are different — is more important. A landless labourer and a prosperous farmer face very different daily realities. A girl from a rich family desires freedom and equality; a tribal desires land rights. These differences arise not from personality but from where people stand in life.

Even the same person in different circumstances would desire different things. So life situations primarily shape developmental goals.

2
Do these two statements mean the same? (a) People have different developmental goals. (b) People have conflicting developmental goals.
Answer

No, these statements do not mean the same.

Different goals: A farmer wants good rains; an urban youth wants a job — different but not conflicting.

Conflicting goals: When one person's development harms another's. Industrialists building dams (development for them) displaces tribals (destruction for them). All conflicting goals are different, but not all different goals are conflicting.

3
Give some examples where factors other than income are important aspects of our lives.
Answer
  • Security: A regular job with less pay may be preferred over a high-paying job with no security.
  • Respect and dignity: Women in paid work gain dignity in the household and society.
  • Freedom: A safe and secure environment allows more women to take up jobs or run businesses.
  • Friendship and relationships: Friends enrich our lives in ways money cannot buy.
  • Equal treatment: Freedom from discrimination matters regardless of income level.
  • Clean environment: Money cannot always buy a pollution-free environment.
  • Community health: Protection from infectious diseases requires community-level preventive steps.
4
Explain some of the important ideas of the above section in your own words.
Answer

Development is not just about income. It is about quality of life, which includes many non-material things. Different people desire different things depending on where they are in life — there is no single idea of development that applies to everyone.

Sometimes, what benefits one group harms another. Building a dam produces electricity for industries but submerges tribal homes. So development goals can conflict.

National development means finding a fair path that benefits the largest number of people — asking what is just and equitable for all.

Let's Work These Out — Average Income (pg 9)
1
Give three examples where an average is used for comparing situations.
Answer
  • Average marks: Class average used to compare performance of different schools.
  • Average rainfall: Annual average used to compare climates of different regions.
  • BMI (Body Mass Index): Uses average expected height and weight to assess health.
  • Per capita income: Average income per person used to compare countries.
2
Why do you think average income is an important criterion for development? Explain.
Answer

Average (per capita) income is important because it allows fair comparison of countries with different population sizes. Higher income generally means people can access more goods and services. It is simple, calculable, and universally accepted — used by the World Bank for country classification.

However, it does not reveal how income is distributed — a country with high average income may still have extreme inequality.

3
Besides size of per capita income, what other property of income is important in comparing two or more societies?
Answer

The distribution of income is equally important. It tells us how equitably income is spread among the population. As shown by the Country A and B example — both have the same average, but Country A (all citizens earning ~₹10,000) is more equitable and preferable to Country B (4 earning ₹500, one earning ₹48,000).

4
Suppose records show that the average income in a country has been increasing over time. Can we conclude that all sections have become better off?
Answer

No. Rising average income can hide inequality. If the rich get richer while the poor stay the same, the average rises — but the majority are no better off. Example: If in Country B the rich person's income rises from ₹48,000 to ₹80,000 while the four poor remain at ₹500 each, the average rises — but 4 out of 5 people see no improvement.

Let's Work These Out — Public Facilities (pg 12)
1
Is Haryana ahead of Kerala in literacy rate etc., as it is in terms of per capita income?
Answer

No, Haryana is NOT ahead of Kerala in human development indicators despite higher income.

  • Literacy Rate: Haryana 82% vs Kerala 94%
  • Infant Mortality Rate: Haryana 28 vs Kerala 6 (nearly 3× worse)
  • Net Attendance Ratio (15-17 yrs): Haryana 73% vs Kerala 94%

This proves that per capita income alone does not determine human well-being.

2
Think of other examples where collective provision of goods and services is cheaper than individual provision.
Answer
  • Fire brigade: One community fire station is cheaper than each family maintaining its own firefighting equipment.
  • Street lighting: Collective street lights are cheaper than individual lanterns for each household.
  • Public transport: Buses and trains reduce per-person cost compared to everyone owning a car.
  • Water supply pipelines: Common supply cheaper than individual borewells for each home.
  • Parks and playgrounds: One shared park benefits thousands at low per-person cost.
  • Vaccination drives: Community vaccination prevents disease for everyone at minimal individual cost.
3
Does availability of good health and educational facilities depend only on money spent by the government? What other factors are relevant?
Answer

No. Other important factors include:

  • Effective utilisation of funds: Money must reach beneficiaries without corruption.
  • Community participation: Awareness and demand ensure facilities are actually used.
  • Quality of facilities: A school without teachers provides little benefit regardless of spending.
  • Social equality: Caste and gender discrimination can prevent access even when facilities exist.
  • Infrastructure: Roads and connectivity help remote areas access services.
  • Political will: Kerala's success is partly due to strong commitment to social welfare.
4
In Tamil Nadu, 90% of rural people use ration shops; in West Bengal only 35% do. Where would people be better off and why?
Answer

People in Tamil Nadu would be better off because the PDS functions far more effectively there. With 90% utilisation, the poor have much better food and nutritional security through subsidised food grains.

In West Bengal, with only 35% using ration shops, most poor people must buy food at market prices, leaving less money for health, education, and other needs.

NCERT Exercises (pg 16–17)
1
Development of a country can generally be determined by (i) its per capita income (ii) its average literacy level (iii) health status of its people (iv) all the above
Correct answer: (iv) All the above

Development depends on multiple factors. Per capita income shows material well-being; literacy shows educational development; health status shows quality of life. All three together give a more complete picture — as reflected in the UNDP's HDI approach.

2
Which of the following neighbouring countries has better performance in terms of human development than India? (i) Bangladesh (ii) Sri Lanka (iii) Nepal (iv) Pakistan
Correct answer: (ii) Sri Lanka

As per HDR 2025 data (for 2023), Sri Lanka has an HDI rank of 89, far better than India's 130 out of 193 countries. Sri Lanka also has higher GNI per capita (PPP $12,616 vs $9,047), higher life expectancy (77.5 vs 72 years), and more mean years of schooling (10.8 vs 6.9 years).

3
Average per capita income of 4 families is ₹5000. Three families earn ₹4000, ₹7000, ₹3000. What is the income of the fourth family? (i) ₹7500 (ii) ₹3000 (iii) ₹2000 (iv) ₹6000
Correct answer: (iv) ₹6000

Calculation: Total income = 5000 × 4 = ₹20,000. Sum of 3 known = 4000 + 7000 + 3000 = ₹14,000. Fourth family = 20,000 − 14,000 = ₹6,000

4
What is the main criterion used by the World Bank in classifying different countries? What are the limitations of this criterion, if any?
Answer

Main criterion: Per capita income (total income ÷ total population). High income ≥ US$66,500/year; Low income ≤ US$2,300/year; India = US$11,000 (lower middle income).

Limitations:

  • Averages hide inequality and income distribution within the country.
  • Does not reflect quality of life — high income can coexist with poor health and education.
  • Ignores non-material aspects like security, freedom, and environmental quality.
  • Does not consider whether income is earned sustainably or by depleting resources.

6
Why do we use averages? Are there any limitations to their use? Illustrate with your own examples related to development.
Answer

Why we use averages: Averages allow comparison of large groups by summarising data into one number. Per capita income allows comparison between countries with different populations.

Limitations:

  • Averages hide disparities. Country A (all earning ~₹10,000) and Country B (4 earning ₹500, one earning ₹48,000) have the same average but very different realities.
  • A rising national average does not mean all groups benefit — the rich getting richer raises the average even if the poor do not improve.
  • Averages of rainfall, BMI, marks, etc., all mask extremes and variation.
7
Kerala, with lower per capita income has a better human development ranking than Haryana. Hence, per capita income is not a useful criterion at all and should not be used. Do you agree? Discuss.
Answer

No, I do not completely agree. The Kerala-Haryana example proves income alone is insufficient. Kerala's superior IMR (6 vs 28), literacy (94% vs 82%), and attendance (94% vs 73%) despite lower income show income is not everything.

However, per capita income should not be abandoned because: more income generally means access to more goods and services; it is easy to measure and compare; and it remains an important starting point used by the World Bank.

Conclusion: Per capita income is necessary but not sufficient. It must be used alongside health, education, and equity indicators for a complete picture.

8
Find out the present sources of energy that are used by the people in India. What could be the other possibilities fifty years from now?
Answer

Present sources: Coal (thermal power), crude oil and natural gas (petrol, diesel, LPG), firewood and biomass (rural areas), hydroelectric power, nuclear energy, solar and wind energy.

Fifty years from now: Solar energy, wind energy (offshore and onshore), hydrogen fuel cells, advanced nuclear (fusion), biogas from organic waste, tidal and ocean energy. India's target of 500 GW of renewable energy by 2030 is a step in this direction.

9
Why is the issue of sustainability important for development?
Answer
  • Resources are finite: Crude oil (~47 years left) and coal will eventually run out.
  • Future generations: "We have borrowed this world from our children." Overuse today leaves less for tomorrow.
  • Renewables can be overused: Groundwater is being overused in 1/3 of India — 60% will face scarcity in 25 years.
  • Global concern: Environmental degradation crosses borders — sustainability requires global action.
  • Irreversible damage: Some ecological damage cannot be undone, permanently limiting future options.
10
"The Earth has enough resources to meet the needs of all but not enough to satisfy the greed of even one person." How is this statement relevant to the discussion of development?
Answer

This statement by Mahatma Gandhi captures the essence of sustainable development. The Earth has enough resources to meet everyone's basic needs if used wisely and equitably. The problem is over-consumption — a small fraction of the world's population consumes a disproportionately large share.

  • If all countries pursue the same resource-intensive path as rich nations, Earth's resources will be exhausted rapidly.
  • The Abidjan toxic waste case shows greed overriding people's well-being.
  • Groundwater overuse in prosperous Punjab depletes what is needed for future generations.

The solution lies in equitable distribution and sustainable consumption patterns.

11
List a few examples of environmental degradation that you may have observed around you.
Answer
  • Air pollution: Smoke from factories, vehicles, and crop stubble burning causing severe smog.
  • Water pollution: Industrial waste and untreated sewage dumped into rivers like Yamuna and Ganga.
  • Groundwater depletion: Excessive borewell drilling has lowered water tables in many districts.
  • Deforestation: Clearing of forests for construction and agriculture destroys biodiversity.
  • Solid waste: Overflowing landfills and plastic waste in public spaces and water bodies.
  • Soil degradation: Excessive chemical fertilisers reduce soil fertility over time.
  • Loss of biodiversity: Encroachment into wildlife habitats threatens many species.

Important Terms to Know

Per Capita Income

Total national income divided by total population. Used to compare average economic well-being across countries.

Infant Mortality Rate (IMR)

Number of children dying before age 1 per 1000 live births in a year. Lower IMR = better development.

Literacy Rate

Proportion of literate persons in the population aged 7 years and above.

Net Attendance Ratio

Percentage of children aged 15–17 attending secondary school out of total children in that age group.

Life Expectancy at Birth

Average expected length of life of a person at the time of birth. A key HDI indicator.

Human Development Index (HDI)

UNDP measure combining per capita income, life expectancy, and mean years of schooling. Ranks 193 countries.

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

Adjusts per capita income so that every dollar buys the same amount of goods & services in any country.

Sustainable Development

Development that meets present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

Renewable Resources

Resources replenished by nature (groundwater, forests). Can still be overused if extraction exceeds replenishment.

Non-Renewable Resources

Resources with a fixed stock that cannot be replenished (crude oil, coal). Will eventually be exhausted.

Public Distribution System (PDS)

Government system providing food grains at subsidised prices to the poor. Effective in Tamil Nadu and Kerala.

National Development

A fair and just path of growth that benefits the largest number of people, considering material and non-material goals.
