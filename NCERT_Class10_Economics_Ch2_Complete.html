Primary, Secondary & Tertiary Sectors
Classification by nature of activity

Primary sector: Activities using natural resources directly — agriculture, dairy, fishing, forestry, mining. Also called agriculture and allied sector. Forms the base for all other products.

Secondary sector: Natural products are changed into other forms through manufacturing — spinning cloth from cotton, making sugar from sugarcane, bricks from earth. Also called industrial sector.

Tertiary sector: Activities that help develop primary and secondary sectors — transport, banking, insurance, trade, communication, storage. Does not produce goods but provides services. Also called service sector.

GDP (Gross Domestic Product) = Sum of value of all final goods and services produced within a country during a particular year. Only final goods are counted to avoid double counting.
Sectors in India — GVA & Employment
The mismatch between production and jobs

Between 1977-78 and 2017-18, production in all three sectors increased, but the tertiary sector grew the most and is now the largest producing sector in India.

However, in employment, the primary sector continues to be the largest employer even now. Over half the workers are in the primary sector, yet it produces only about 1/6th of GVA.

Key mismatch: More people in primary sector → low productivity per person → underemployment. Tertiary sector produces most but employs relatively fewer people.

Disguised unemployment (hidden unemployment): When people are apparently working but all are working less than their potential. Common in agriculture — a family farm with 5 members when only 3 are needed.

Organised vs Unorganised Sectors
Kanta and Kamal — a tale of two workers

Organised (Kanta)

  • Fixed working hours
  • Regular salary
  • Provident fund, gratuity
  • Paid leave & holidays
  • Medical benefits
  • Appointment letter
  • Job security

Unorganised (Kamal)

  • No fixed hours (7:30am–8pm)
  • Daily wages only
  • No provident fund
  • No paid leave
  • No medical benefits
  • No formal appointment
  • Can be dismissed anytime
Most workers from SC, ST, and backward communities are in the unorganised sector. They face both economic exploitation and social discrimination. About 93% of India's workforce is in the unorganised sector.
Public vs Private Sectors
Who owns it and why it matters

Public sector: Government owns assets and provides services. Motive is not just profit but social welfare. Examples: Indian Railways, post office, BHEL, ONGC.

Private sector: Ownership and delivery in hands of private individuals or companies. Motive is to earn profits. Examples: Tata Iron and Steel (TISCO), Reliance Industries (RIL).

Why is government involvement needed?
1. Some activities need huge investment beyond private capacity (roads, dams, railways)
2. Collecting fees from millions of users is not easy
3. Private sector would charge very high prices
4. Some activities are primary responsibilities of the state — healthcare, education, food security

2
Explain the difference between primary, secondary and tertiary sectors using examples other than those in the text.
Answer

Primary sector: Activities that use natural resources directly. Example — fishing (uses water and fish from nature), mining iron ore (exploiting natural mineral deposits), silk farming (depends on mulberry plants and silkworms).

Secondary sector: Natural products are changed into manufactured goods. Example — making steel from iron ore, producing silk fabric from silk threads, converting milk (primary) into cheese or butter (secondary), processing raw rubber into tyres.

Tertiary sector: Services that support primary and secondary sectors. Example — a bank giving loans to a farmer (financial service), a hospital treating a factory worker (health service), a courier delivering manufactured goods (transport service), a teacher in a school (educational service).

4
Students in a school are often classified into primary and secondary or junior and senior. What is the criterion used? Do you think this is a useful classification?
Answer

The criterion used is age or class/grade level — primary (Classes 1-5, younger students) and secondary (Classes 6-10/12, older students).

Yes, this is a useful classification because: different age groups have different learning needs, teaching methods, and curriculum levels. Primary students need simpler concepts and more play-based learning, while secondary students can handle abstract and analytical thinking. Different teachers, different facilities, and different assessment methods can be designed for each group.

Similarly, in economics, classifying sectors helps us understand the nature, production, and employment patterns of each group to design appropriate policies.

Let's Work These Out — GDP & Historical Change (pg 23)
1
What does the history of developed countries indicate about the shifts that have taken place between sectors?
Answer

The history of developed countries shows a clear sequential shift between sectors over time:

  • Stage 1 (Early development): Primary sector dominated — most production and employment was in agriculture and natural resource activities.
  • Stage 2 (Industrialisation): Secondary sector became dominant — factories expanded, people moved from farms to factories, manufacturing became the largest source of production and employment.
  • Stage 3 (Post-industrialisation): In the past 100 years, there has been a shift from secondary to tertiary — the service sector became the most important in terms of both total production and employment. This is the general pattern in developed countries today.
2
Correct and arrange the important aspects for calculating GDP from this jumble: "To count goods and services we add the numbers that are produced. We count all those that were produced in the last five years. Since we shouldn't leave out anything we add up all these goods and services."
Answer

The corrected version: "To count goods and services, we use their monetary value (not numbers). We count only those that were produced in the current year (not five years). Since we should avoid double counting, we add up only the final goods and services (not intermediate ones)."

Key corrections: (1) Use value (money), not numbers — you can't add cars and computers meaningfully. (2) Count only current year's production. (3) Count only final goods and services, not intermediate goods, to avoid double counting.

3
Compare and contrast changes in India with the pattern observed for developed countries. What kind of changes were desired but did not happen in India?
Answer

Similarity with developed countries: Like developed countries, India's tertiary sector has become the largest contributor to GVA, replacing the primary sector in production terms.

Key difference — what did NOT happen in India:

  • In developed countries, as production shifted from primary → secondary → tertiary, employment also shifted in the same direction. Workers moved out of agriculture into industry and then services.
  • In India, production shifted to tertiary, but employment did NOT shift out of primary sector to the same extent. Primary sector still employs 44% of workers but contributes only ~17% of GVA.
  • Not enough jobs were created in secondary and tertiary sectors to absorb workers from agriculture.

Desired change that didn't happen: Shift of workers from agriculture to better-paying secondary and tertiary sector jobs — leading to a large underemployment problem in India.

4
Why should we be worried about underemployment?
Answer

We should be worried about underemployment because:

  • It means workers are not using their full productive capacity — wastage of human resources.
  • Underemployed workers earn very low incomes, perpetuating poverty.
  • It is hidden (disguised) — not visible in unemployment statistics, so the true extent of the problem is underestimated.
  • It reduces overall national productivity and GDP growth.
  • Families remain trapped in low-income agriculture with no path to higher earnings.
  • It contributes to rural-urban migration as people seek real employment — causing urban overcrowding and straining city infrastructure.
Let's Work These Out — Employment Creation (pg 29)
1
Why do you think MGNREGA 2005 or Viksit Bharat-G RAM G 2025 is referred to as 'Right to Work'?
Answer

MGNREGA 2005 / Viksit Bharat-G RAM G 2025 is called 'Right to Work' because it makes employment a legal entitlement rather than just a policy aim:

  • It guarantees 100 days of employment per year to all rural adults who are able to work and need it — making work a right, not a favour.
  • If the government fails to provide employment, it must pay an unemployment allowance — holding the government accountable.
  • It is backed by law — anyone can demand work under this Act and the government is legally obligated to provide it.

This is similar to how Right to Education makes schooling a legal right — it transforms a government duty into a citizen's right that can be claimed and enforced.

2
Imagine you are the village head. Suggest some activities that should be taken up under this Act that would also increase the income of people.
Answer

As village head, I would suggest the following activities under MGNREGA/Viksit Bharat-G RAM G:

  • Construction of irrigation canals and check dams — provides employment now and increases agricultural productivity later
  • Building and repairing rural roads — connects villages to markets, reducing post-harvest losses
  • Watershed development and soil conservation — improves land productivity for future agriculture
  • Afforestation and planting of fruit trees — employment now, income from fruits later
  • Construction of community ponds and rainwater harvesting structures — reduces dependence on monsoon
  • Building school rooms and village health centres — improves social infrastructure while creating jobs
3
How would income and employment increase if farmers were provided with irrigation and marketing facilities?
Answer

Irrigation facilities would:

  • Allow farmers to grow a second or even third crop in a year (like wheat in rabi season), directly creating more employment in cultivation
  • Increase agricultural output, raising farm income
  • Make farming less risky and more reliable, encouraging investment

Marketing facilities would:

  • Allow farmers to sell directly at better prices instead of being forced to sell cheaply to local traders immediately after harvest
  • Create employment in transport, storage (cold storage), and trade
  • Enable farmers to wait for better prices through proper storage (cold chains)
  • Reduce post-harvest losses and food wastage

Together, irrigation + marketing can multiply income and create jobs along the entire agricultural value chain.

4
In what ways can employment be increased in urban areas?
Answer
  • Expanding education sector: Build more schools, colleges — creates jobs for teachers, staff, and support services
  • Expanding health sector: More hospitals, clinics, health centres need doctors, nurses, paramedics
  • Developing IT and new services: Software companies, call centres, internet services — high employment potential
  • Tourism development: More tourists means more hotels, guides, transport, restaurants, souvenir shops
  • Small-scale industries: Government support for micro and small enterprises in urban areas
  • Vocational training programmes: Skill development for urban youth makes them employable in organised sector
  • Infrastructure development: Urban construction, metro projects, smart city initiatives generate employment

3
How would you distinguish between organised and unorganised sectors? Explain in your own words.
Answer

The organised sector consists of enterprises registered with the government that follow rules and regulations (Factories Act, Minimum Wages Act, etc.). Workers have assured, regular employment with fixed hours, paid leave, provident fund, gratuity, and other benefits. They have appointment letters and job security.

The unorganised sector consists of small, scattered units largely outside government control. Even where rules exist, they are not followed. Jobs are low-paid, irregular, and insecure. There are no benefits like paid leave, provident fund, or medical facilities. Workers can be asked to leave anytime.

Example: Kanta (office worker with salary, PF, and holidays) works in the organised sector. Kamal (daily wage labourer in a grocery shop, no benefits, no security) works in the unorganised sector.

6
Do you think the classification of economic activities into primary, secondary and tertiary is useful? Explain how.
Answer

Yes, this classification is very useful because:

  • It helps us understand the structure of the economy — how much each sector contributes to GVA/GDP and how many people work in each sector.
  • It reveals historical trends — how economies shift from primary to secondary to tertiary over time, helping us understand the stage of development.
  • It identifies imbalances — in India, the mismatch (primary sector employs 44% but contributes only 17% to GVA) highlights underemployment and helps formulate policies.
  • It helps target government policies — knowing where unemployment or underemployment is concentrated allows specific interventions.
  • It helps in international comparisons — countries at similar development levels show similar sectoral patterns.
7
For each sector, why should one focus on employment and GVA? Could there be other issues to examine?
Answer

Employment and GVA are focused on because they show two dimensions of a sector's importance: how much it produces (GVA) and how many people it supports (employment). The mismatch between these two reveals underemployment and productivity problems.

Other important issues to examine:

  • Quality of employment — are jobs secure and well-paying, or precarious and low-wage?
  • Gender participation — are women equally represented in each sector?
  • Environmental impact — is the sector's growth sustainable?
  • Regional distribution — is growth concentrated in some states and absent in others?
  • Wages and income inequality within each sector
  • Working conditions — safety, hours, benefits
9
How is the tertiary sector different from other sectors? Illustrate with a few examples.
Answer

The tertiary (service) sector is different because it does not produce physical goods — it provides services that help other sectors function and satisfies consumer needs directly.

  • Transport: Trucks and railways move agricultural produce from farms (primary) to factories (secondary) and markets — without producing a new good.
  • Banking: A bank lending money to a farmer or factory owner facilitates production — but the bank itself doesn't grow crops or make goods.
  • Education: A teacher creates human capital — but doesn't produce any physical product.
  • IT services: A software company like Infosys writes code and provides solutions — entirely service-based with no tangible product.
  • Healthcare: A doctor restores health — a critical service with no physical output.
10
What do you understand by disguised unemployment? Explain with an example each from urban and rural areas.
Answer

Disguised unemployment (also called hidden unemployment) is when people are apparently working but are working less than their potential. Their removal would not affect total production. It is the opposite of open unemployment where people are visibly jobless.

Rural example: Laxmi's family of 5 members all work on a 2-hectare unirrigated farm. The farm actually needs only 3 workers. The other 2 appear to be employed but contribute little to actual output. If they left, farm production would not fall. Their labour effort is simply divided among too many people.

Urban example: A small road-side shop has the owner and 3 family members serving customers. The shop actually needs only 1-2 people. The extra 2 family members appear busy but could leave without any reduction in the shop's sales or income. They are disguisedly unemployed.

12
"Tertiary sector is not playing any significant role in the development of Indian economy." Do you agree? Give reasons.
Answer

No, I strongly disagree. The tertiary sector plays a very significant role in India's development:

  • It is the largest contributor to GVA in 2017-18 (~54%), having overtaken the primary sector — clearly the most important sector in production terms.
  • It provides basic services essential for the economy — banking, transport, communication, healthcare, education — without which primary and secondary sectors cannot function.
  • IT, software, and business process outsourcing (BPO) have made India globally competitive and generated substantial foreign exchange earnings.
  • It absorbs educated and skilled workers from both urban and rural areas.

13
Service sector in India employs two different kinds of people. Who are these?
Answer

The service sector in India employs two very different kinds of people at opposite ends of the spectrum:

1. Highly skilled and educated workers (small number): Doctors, software engineers, lawyers, management consultants, chartered accountants, IT professionals. They are well-paid and work in organised enterprises. This is the growing, modern part of the service sector.

2. Low-skilled workers doing small jobs (very large number): Small shopkeepers, repair persons (electricians, plumbers), pushcart vendors, street sellers, domestic workers, transport persons. They earn very little, barely manage a living, and work in the unorganised sector because they have no alternative opportunities. They are "service workers" by necessity, not by choice.

14
Workers are exploited in the unorganised sector. Do you agree? Give reasons.
Answer

Yes, workers in the unorganised sector are exploited. Evidence:

  • Low wages: Workers earn below minimum wage in many cases; employers evade the Minimum Wages Act.
  • No job security: Workers can be dismissed without notice or reason, especially in lean seasons.
  • Long working hours: Like Kamal (7:30am to 8pm) — far beyond the legal 8-hour limit with no overtime pay.
  • No benefits: No provident fund, no gratuity, no medical benefits, no paid leave — all of which are legal entitlements in the organised sector.
  • Social discrimination: Workers from SC, ST, and OBC communities face additional discrimination on top of economic exploitation.
  • Unsafe conditions: Many small workshops do not follow safety standards — workers suffer injuries with no compensation.
15
How are the activities in the economy classified on the basis of employment conditions?
Answer

On the basis of employment conditions, economic activities are classified into organised and unorganised sectors:

Organised sector: Enterprises registered with government; follow laws (Factories Act, Minimum Wages Act, Payment of Gratuity Act, etc.); workers have regular, assured employment; fixed working hours; paid leave; PF, gratuity, medical benefits; appointment letters; job security; pension after retirement.

Unorganised sector: Small, scattered units; largely outside government control; rules not followed; jobs are low-paid, irregular, insecure; no overtime pay; no leave benefits; no PF; no formal appointment; workers can be dismissed anytime; no pension. Includes most agricultural workers, small shopkeepers, domestic workers, street vendors, construction labourers.

17
Explain the objective of implementing MGNREGA 2005 or Viksit Bharat-G RAM G 2025.
Answer

Key objectives:

  • Guarantee employment: Provide 100 days of guaranteed wage employment per year to rural households whose adult members are willing to do unskilled manual work.
  • Right to work: Make employment a legal right — if the government fails to provide work, it must pay an unemployment allowance.
  • Reduce rural underemployment and poverty: Provide income support to the rural poor, especially during lean agricultural seasons.
  • Create productive assets: Work taken up should help increase future agricultural production — irrigation canals, check dams, rural roads, land development.
  • Empower women and marginalised groups: At least 1/3rd of the workers must be women; SC/ST communities are prioritised.

In 2025, Viksit Bharat-G RAM G 2025 replaced MGNREGA with a broader focus on sustainable livelihoods and rural development.

20
Give a few examples of public sector activities and explain why the government has taken them up.
Answer

Examples of public sector activities and reasons:

  • Indian Railways: Requires enormous investment beyond private capacity; millions of passengers need affordable transport; essential for national integration.
  • ONGC (Oil and Natural Gas Corporation): Strategic resource; too important to leave to private profit motive; ensures energy security.
  • Government schools and hospitals: Providing education and healthcare to all, especially the poor, is a government responsibility — private sector would charge unaffordable rates.
  • Construction of dams and canals: Massive investment needed; benefits spread across millions of farmers; private sector cannot recover costs.
  • Post offices: Provide communication services even in remote areas where private operators find it unprofitable.
  • Public Distribution System (PDS): Ensures food security for the poor by subsidising food grains — private sector would not do this.
21
Explain how the public sector contributes to the economic development of a nation.
Answer

The public sector contributes to economic development in multiple ways:

  • Infrastructure development: Builds roads, railways, ports, power plants, irrigation dams — all of which reduce production costs for private businesses and enable economic activity.
  • Human capital development: Public schools and hospitals build a healthy, educated workforce — the foundation of long-term growth.
  • Price stability: Government procures and distributes essential goods (wheat, rice through PDS) to prevent price shocks for the poor.
  • Employment generation: Government is one of the largest employers — creating jobs in education, health, defence, administration.
  • Regional balance: Invests in backward regions that the private sector ignores, reducing regional inequalities.
  • Supporting private sector: Provides electricity at subsidised rates, enabling small industries to operate and grow.
  • Social welfare: Programmes like MGNREGA/Viksit Bharat-G RAM G support the poor and prevent social instability.
22
Workers in the unorganised sector need protection on wages, safety and health. Explain with examples.
Answer

Wages: Many unorganised sector workers are paid below minimum wage. Example — a construction labourer hired daily may be paid less than the legally mandated minimum wage because the contractor exploits their lack of knowledge of their rights. Government needs to strictly enforce the Minimum Wages Act.

Safety: Workers in small workshops, mines, and construction sites often work without safety equipment. Example — a worker in a small fireworks factory near Sivakasi works with highly flammable materials without proper protective gear — factory explosions are common. Safety inspections must be mandatory and regular.

Health: Workers have no medical benefits. Example — a domestic worker who falls ill has no sick leave and no income during illness. If the illness is serious, she may have to borrow from a moneylender at high interest. Government health schemes (like Ayushman Bharat) should cover all unorganised sector workers.

Important Terms to Know

Primary Sector

Activities that directly use natural resources — farming, dairy, fishing, forestry, mining. Also called agriculture and allied sector. Forms the base for all products.

Secondary Sector

Activities that convert natural products into manufactured goods — spinning, weaving, sugar making, brick making. Also called industrial sector.

Tertiary Sector

Activities that provide services to primary and secondary sectors — transport, banking, trade, insurance, communication, IT. Also called service sector.

GDP (Gross Domestic Product)

Value of all final goods and services produced within a country during a particular year. Measures how big the economy is.

GVA (Gross Value Added)

Measures contribution of three sectors after adjusting for taxes and subsidies. India now uses GVA in place of GDP for sector comparisons.

Final Goods

Goods that reach the end consumer. Only final goods are counted in GDP to avoid double counting of intermediate goods used in production.

Intermediate Goods

Goods used in producing other goods (e.g., wheat flour used to make biscuits). Not counted separately in GDP as their value is included in the final good.

Underemployment

When people are apparently working but working less than their potential. Their removal would not reduce production. Also called disguised unemployment.

Disguised Unemployment

Hidden underemployment — looks like employment but excess workers contribute nothing to output. Common in Indian agriculture (e.g., 5 people on a farm needing only 3).

Organised Sector

Enterprises registered with government; follow labour laws; workers have regular employment, fixed hours, provident fund, paid leave, job security, and appointment letters.

Unorganised Sector

Small, scattered units outside government control; rules not followed; jobs are low-paid, irregular, insecure; no benefits — covers ~83% of India's workforce.

Public Sector

Government owns assets and provides services. Motive is welfare, not profit. Examples: Indian Railways, Post Office, ONGC, BHEL.

Private Sector

Ownership and services in hands of private individuals or companies. Motive is profit. Examples: Tata Iron & Steel (TISCO), Reliance Industries (RIL).

MGNREGA 2005

Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act — guaranteed 100 days of employment per year to rural adults. Replaced in 2025 by Viksit Bharat-G RAM G 2025.

Viksit Bharat-G RAM G 2025

Replaced MGNREGA in 2025. Guarantee for Rozgar and Ajeevika Mission — focused on sustainable rural livelihoods and employment generation.

Important Facts for Exams
  • The three types of sector classifications: (1) Primary/Secondary/Tertiary — nature of activity; (2) Organised/Unorganised — employment conditions; (3) Public/Private — ownership.
  • In 2017-18, the tertiary sector is the largest producer in India (replaced primary sector). Primary sector was largest in 1977-78.
  • Despite producing most GVA, the primary sector still employs ~44% of India's workers — the largest employer.
  • Agricultural sector employs more than half the workers but produces only about one-sixth of GVA — indicating widespread underemployment.
  • ~83% of India's workers are in the unorganised sector. Only ~17% are in the organised sector.
  • MGNREGA 2005 guaranteed 100 days of employment per year to rural adults. Replaced by Viksit Bharat-G RAM G 2025.
  • Tourism can create employment for over 35 lakh people annually. Education sector alone can create 20 lakh jobs (NITI Aayog estimate).
  • Kanta (organised sector) vs Kamal (unorganised sector) — the textbook's key example comparing employment conditions.
  • Only final goods and services are counted in GDP — not intermediate goods — to avoid double counting.
  • Public sector examples: Indian Railways, Post Office, ONGC, BHEL, MTNL, All India Radio, Air India. Private sector: TISCO (Tata), Reliance Industries, Jet Airways.
  • Farmers who own 2 hectares with 5 family members — Laxmi — is the textbook's key example of disguised unemployment in agriculture.
  • Nearly 80% of rural households in India are in the small and marginal farmer category.
