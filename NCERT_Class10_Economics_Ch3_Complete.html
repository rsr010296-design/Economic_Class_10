Money as a Medium of Exchange
Solving the barter problem

In a barter system, goods are directly exchanged without money. This requires double coincidence of wants — what you want to sell is exactly what the other person wants to buy, AND vice versa. This is extremely difficult to arrange.

Money eliminates double coincidence of wants by acting as an intermediate step. A shoe manufacturer sells shoes for money, then uses that money to buy wheat — without needing to find a wheat farmer who also wants shoes.

Modern forms of money: Currency (paper notes and coins) issued by RBI on behalf of the central government. Modern currency has no intrinsic value — it is accepted because it is authorised by law. No one in India can legally refuse a payment in rupees.

Demand deposits: Money deposited in bank accounts. Can be withdrawn on demand. Cheques allow direct settlement of payments without cash — so demand deposits also function as money.

Loan Activities of Banks
How banks mediate between depositors and borrowers

Banks accept deposits from the public and pay interest on them. They keep only a small proportion (~5% in India) as cash reserve and use the major portion to extend loans.

Banks mediate between depositors (surplus funds) and borrowers (need funds). They charge a higher interest rate on loans than they offer on deposits — this difference is their main source of income.

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) supervises all formal sector lending — ensuring banks maintain cash balance, lending to priority sectors, and submitting periodic reports.

Terms of Credit & Two Credit Situations
Salim (positive) vs Swapna (debt trap)

Terms of credit = Interest rate + Collateral & documentation requirements + Mode of repayment

Collateral = Asset owned by borrower (land, building, vehicle, livestock, bank deposits) used as guarantee. If loan is not repaid, lender can sell the collateral.

Salim (positive credit): Got a loan to meet working capital needs → delivered order on time → made profit → repaid loan. Credit helped increase earnings.

Swapna (debt trap): Crop failed → couldn't repay moneylender → debt grew → had to sell part of land. Credit pushed her into a debt trap.

Whether credit is helpful or harmful depends on the risks in the situation and whether there is support in case of loss.

Formal vs Informal Credit & SHGs
Who gets what and why the poor are excluded

Formal sector: Banks and cooperatives — supervised by RBI, lower interest rates. Informal sector: Moneylenders, traders, employers, relatives — no supervision, very high interest rates.

As of 2019: Commercial banks provide 51% of rural credit. Moneylenders provide 23%. The poor depend more on informal sources (54% of poor households' loans from informal sources vs only 17% for rich households).

Self-Help Groups (SHGs): 15-20 members (mainly women) pool savings (₹25-100/month), lend to members at reasonable rates, and after 1-2 years become eligible for bank loans as a group. Overcomes the collateral problem. Promotes financial self-reliance and addresses social issues (health, nutrition, domestic violence).
Let's Work These Out — Money as Medium of Exchange (pg 40)
1
How does the use of money make it easier to exchange things?
Answer

Money makes exchange easier by eliminating the need for double coincidence of wants — the major problem with barter.

In a barter system, a shoe manufacturer who wants wheat must find a wheat farmer who not only wants to sell wheat but also wants to buy shoes. This simultaneous matching is very difficult to arrange.

With money, the shoe manufacturer simply: (1) sells shoes to anyone who wants them, receiving money; and (2) uses that money to buy wheat from any wheat seller. Money acts as a medium of exchange — an intermediate step that separates the two transactions and makes them independent of each other.

Everyone prefers to receive payments in money because money can be exchanged for any commodity or service — it has universal acceptability.

2
Can you think of some examples of goods/services being exchanged or wages being paid through barter?
Answer
  • Agricultural labour paid in grain: In some villages, agricultural labourers are paid in kind — they receive a share of the crop (wheat, rice) instead of cash wages.
  • Doctor treated in exchange for food: A village doctor may treat a patient and receive food items or vegetables in return.
  • Carpenter-farmer exchange: A carpenter builds furniture for a farmer and receives grain in return.
  • Skill exchange: A teacher tutors a carpenter's children; the carpenter makes furniture for the teacher — no money changes hands.
  • Online barter platforms: Modern websites exist where people exchange goods/services directly — old books for clothes, photography skills for cooking lessons, etc.
Let's Work These Out — Cheques & Demand Deposits (pg 42)
1
M. Salim wants to withdraw ₹20,000 in cash. How would he write a cheque to withdraw money?
Answer

To withdraw cash using a cheque, Salim would write a self-cheque (a cheque drawn in his own name or marked "Self") with the following details:

  • Payee: "Self" or his own name (M. Salim)
  • Amount in figures: ₹20,000/-
  • Amount in words: "Twenty Thousand Rupees Only"
  • Date: Current date
  • Signature: His authorised signature
  • Account number: His bank account number

He would then present this cheque at his bank branch or at the bank's counter. The bank will verify his identity and signature, and pay him ₹20,000 in cash, debiting his account.

2
After the transaction between Salim and Prem, which is correct? (i) Both balances increase (ii) Salim's decreases, Prem's increases (iii) Salim's increases, Prem's decreases
Correct answer: (ii) Salim's balance decreases and Prem's balance increases

When Salim writes a cheque to pay Prem (the leather supplier), Salim instructs his bank to pay a specific amount to Prem. This amount is debited (subtracted) from Salim's account and credited (added) to Prem's account. Total money in the system remains the same — it just moves from one account to another.

3
Why are demand deposits considered as money?
Answer

Demand deposits are considered money because they share the essential features of money:

  • Medium of exchange: Payments can be made directly through cheques drawn against demand deposits — without the use of cash.
  • Withdrawable on demand: Unlike fixed deposits, demand deposits can be withdrawn at any time, making them as liquid as cash.
  • Widely accepted: Cheques against demand deposits are accepted as a means of payment by businesses, individuals, and institutions.
  • No cash needed: The cheque payment between Salim and Prem was completed without any actual transfer of cash — just an entry in two bank accounts.

Since demand deposits along with currency constitute the modern forms of money, they are closely linked to the working of the modern banking system.

2
Supposing Salim continues to get orders from traders. What would be his position after 6 years?
Answer

If Salim continues to get regular orders from traders, his position after 6 years would be very good:

  • He would have a steady, growing business with regular income from shoe manufacturing.
  • He would repay each loan promptly after fulfilling each order, building a good credit history.
  • His credit worthiness would improve — banks and suppliers would be more willing to give him larger credit at better terms.
  • He could expand his business — hire more workers, buy machinery, take on larger orders.
  • His savings and assets would grow, providing a financial cushion for any future uncertainties.

This shows how productive credit, when backed by stable income and manageable risk, is a powerful tool for economic growth.

3
What are the reasons that make Swapna's situation so risky? Discuss — pesticides, role of moneylenders, climate.
Answer
  • Climate risk: Rain-fed agriculture is highly vulnerable to drought, floods, and irregular monsoons. Swapna's crop failed due to pests — any natural calamity can wipe out the entire crop and income, leaving her unable to repay.
  • Role of moneylenders: Moneylenders charge extremely high interest rates (often 5% per month = 60% per year). When Swapna cannot repay, the debt compounds rapidly into a much larger amount. Moneylenders may harass poor borrowers and have no legal oversight.
  • Pesticides: Even after spending on expensive pesticides, the crop was not saved — adding to costs without salvaging the harvest. This shows that investments in inputs don't always guarantee returns in farming.
  • Lack of collateral: Small farmers have limited assets. When in a debt trap, they may have to sell land — their only productive asset — to repay, leaving them even poorer and unable to farm independently in future.
  • No safety net: There is no crop insurance or government support for Swapna. Without such support, any crop failure becomes financially catastrophic.

1
Why do lenders ask for collateral while lending?
Answer

Lenders ask for collateral as a security/guarantee against the risk of loan default. The reasons are:

  • Protection against default: If the borrower fails to repay, the lender has the legal right to sell the collateral asset to recover the outstanding loan amount.
  • Ensures commitment: Pledging an important asset (like land or house) gives the borrower a strong incentive to repay — they risk losing the asset if they don't.
  • Risk assessment: Collateral provides the lender with some assurance about the borrower's creditworthiness and ability to repay.
  • Bank security regulations: Banks are required by RBI to maintain standards — collateral ensures loans are backed by real assets.
2
Given that a large number of people in our country are poor, does it affect their capacity to borrow?
Answer

Yes, poverty severely limits people's capacity to borrow from formal sources.

  • No collateral: The poor have few assets to offer as security. Banks require collateral — which the poor typically lack — making it impossible to get formal bank loans.
  • No documentation: Poor workers often lack formal employment records, salary slips, or income proof that banks require.
  • No credit history: Without prior banking relationships, the poor cannot demonstrate creditworthiness.
  • Forced into informal sector: Unable to access bank loans, the poor must borrow from moneylenders at very high interest rates — often leading to debt traps.

This is a vicious cycle: poverty prevents access to cheap formal credit → informal credit with high interest makes the poor poorer → which perpetuates poverty. Breaking this cycle requires innovative solutions like SHGs and expanding formal credit to rural areas.

3
Fill in the blanks: Borrowers look for easy terms — ___ (low/high) interest rate, ___ (easy/tough) repayment conditions, ___ (less/more) collateral and documentation.
Answers: low interest rate, easy conditions for repayment, less collateral and documentation requirements

Borrowers naturally want the most favourable terms — minimum cost (low interest), maximum flexibility (easy repayment), and minimum risk (less collateral to pledge). This is why formal sector loans from banks and cooperatives are so much more desirable than informal sector loans which typically have high interest, harsh repayment terms, and risk of harassment.

2
Why should credit at reasonable rates be available for all?
Answer

Credit at reasonable rates should be available to all because:

  • Essential for development: Credit enables farmers to grow crops, businesses to expand, and individuals to invest — all essential for economic development.
  • High-cost credit leads to debt traps: When interest rates are too high (as with informal lenders), borrowers spend most of their income on repayment, leaving little for growth. It can push people like Swapna into debt traps.
  • Cheap credit = more enterprise: At low interest rates, more people can afford to start businesses, expand production, and create employment.
  • Reduces inequality: Currently the rich get cheap formal credit while the poor pay high informal rates — this widens the income gap. Equal access to cheap credit is essential for equitable development.
  • Right of the people: As the chapter emphasises, access to reasonable credit is a right and its absence keeps large sections outside the development process.
3
Should there be a supervisor for informal lenders like RBI? Why would its task be quite difficult?
Answer

Yes, there should be a supervisor for informal lenders to protect poor borrowers from exploitation, ensure fair interest rates, and maintain records of transactions.

Why the task would be difficult:

  • Huge numbers: Informal lenders include millions of moneylenders, traders, employers, and individuals spread across every village and town in India.
  • No formal registration: Unlike banks, informal lenders are not registered and operate informally — making them hard to identify and track.
  • No paper trails: Transactions are often verbal with no written records — making supervision and verification nearly impossible.
  • Remote locations: Many informal lenders operate in rural and tribal areas with poor connectivity, making physical oversight impractical.
  • Resistance: Lenders and sometimes even borrowers would resist external intervention as they depend on these relationships.
4
Why is the share of formal sector credit higher for richer households compared to poorer households?
Answer

Rich households access more formal credit because:

  • They have collateral: Rich households own land, buildings, vehicles, and other assets that banks accept as security — the primary requirement for formal loans.
  • They have documentation: Salary slips, income tax returns, employment records — all required by banks — are readily available with richer households.
  • They have credit history: Prior banking relationships and timely repayments make banks more willing to lend.
  • They are in urban areas: Formal bank branches are more accessible in cities where richer households tend to live.
  • They understand the process: Education and financial literacy help them navigate the banking system.

Poor households lack all of the above — forcing them into expensive informal credit. As a result, 54% of poor urban household loans are from informal sources vs only 17% for rich households.

NCERT Exercises (pg 52–53)
1
In situations with high risks, credit might create further problems for the borrower. Explain.
Answer

In high-risk situations, credit can trap borrowers in a debt spiral rather than helping them:

Swapna's example: She borrowed from a moneylender for cultivation. When the crop failed due to pests, she couldn't repay. The debt grew with compounding high interest. The next year, even with a normal crop, earnings were insufficient to cover the old loan. She was forced to sell part of her land to repay — leaving her with less land and less ability to earn in future.

Why high-risk situations are dangerous:

  • The loan must be repaid regardless of whether the activity succeeds — unlike equity investment.
  • With informal lenders charging very high interest (60%+ per year), even a short delay causes the debt to balloon.
  • If the borrower has pledged collateral, failure means losing that asset — potentially their most important productive asset (like land).
  • Once in a debt trap, the borrower must take fresh loans to repay old ones — getting deeper into debt each cycle.

This is why credit in high-risk situations (like rain-dependent farming without insurance) requires careful risk assessment and should ideally be backed by insurance or government support.

2
How does money solve the problem of double coincidence of wants? Explain with an example of your own.
Answer

Double coincidence of wants = a situation in a barter system where what the seller wants to sell is exactly what the buyer wants to buy, AND the buyer has what the seller wants. Both conditions must be satisfied simultaneously.

Example: A potter wants to sell pots and buy rice. In a barter system, she would need to find a rice farmer who not only wants to sell rice but also specifically needs pots. If the rice farmer needs a plough, not pots, no exchange can take place even though both parties want to trade.

How money solves this: With money, the potter sells her pots to anyone who needs them, receives money. She then uses that money to buy rice from any rice seller. The two transactions are now completely independent:

  • She doesn't need the rice farmer to want pots.
  • The rice farmer doesn't need to want pots to sell rice.
  • Money acts as the universal intermediate — eliminating the need for double coincidence of wants entirely.
3
How do banks mediate between those who have surplus money and those who need money?
Answer

Banks act as financial intermediaries in the following way:

  • Accept deposits: People with surplus money (depositors) open bank accounts and deposit their extra cash. Banks pay them interest on these deposits — incentivising saving.
  • Maintain cash reserve: Banks keep only about 5% of deposits as cash for withdrawals by depositors who need money on any given day.
  • Extend loans: Banks use the major portion (remaining ~95%) of deposits to give loans to borrowers — farmers, businesses, students, home buyers, etc.
  • Charge differential interest: Banks charge a higher interest rate on loans than they pay to depositors. This difference is their income and incentive to operate.

In this way, banks efficiently channel idle savings from those who have surplus (depositors) to those who need funds for productive activities (borrowers) — enabling economic activity that would not otherwise be possible.

4
Look at a 10 rupee note. What is written on top? Can you explain this statement?
Answer

On a ₹10 note, on top is written: "भारतीय रिज़र्व बैंक / Reserve Bank of India" and below the central promise is "मैं धारक को दस रुपये अदा करने का वचन देता हूँ / I promise to pay the bearer the sum of Ten Rupees" — signed by the Governor of the Reserve Bank of India.

Explanation:

  • This statement reflects that the Reserve Bank of India, on behalf of the Central Government of India, promises to pay the holder of the note the value of ₹10.
  • It is a legal guarantee — the note is legal tender, meaning no one in India can legally refuse it as payment.
  • The RBI is the sole authority to issue currency in India as per Indian law.
  • Modern currency has no intrinsic value — a paper note is not made of gold or silver. But it is accepted because it has the government's authorisation and legal backing.
  • This is why modern currency is called fiat money — it has value because the government declares it to be legal tender.
5
Why do we need to expand formal sources of credit in India?
Answer

We need to expand formal sources of credit because:

  • Formal sector meets only half the rural credit needs: The remaining credit needs are still met by expensive informal sources. This must change.
  • Lower cost of borrowing: Formal credit (bank at 8-12% p.a.) is far cheaper than informal credit (moneylenders at 60%+ p.a.). Lower interest means more income stays with the borrower.
  • Prevent debt traps: High informal interest rates cause debt traps like Swapna's. Cheap formal credit reduces this risk.
  • Enable more productive investment: Cheap credit allows farmers, small businesses, and entrepreneurs to invest, grow, and create employment.
  • Reduce inequality: Currently, the rich benefit from cheap formal credit while the poor are stuck with expensive informal credit. Expanding formal credit to the poor promotes equitable development.
  • Supervised and fair: Formal sector lenders are regulated — they cannot use unfair or illegal means to recover money, protecting borrowers.
6
What is the basic idea behind the SHGs for the poor? Explain in your own words.
Answer

The basic idea behind Self-Help Groups (SHGs) is to organise the rural poor — especially women — into small groups that pool their savings and help each other access credit, thereby overcoming the main barrier to formal credit: lack of collateral.

How it works:

  • 15-20 members from the same neighbourhood form a group and meet regularly.
  • Each member saves a small amount (₹25-100/month) regularly — collectively building a pool of funds.
  • Members can borrow from this pool at reasonable interest rates (less than moneylenders).
  • After 1-2 years of regular saving, the group becomes eligible for a bank loan (sanctioned in the group's name).
  • Decisions about loans — amount, purpose, interest — are made democratically by group members themselves.
  • The group collectively ensures repayment — peer pressure reduces default risk, which is why banks are willing to lend without collateral.

Beyond credit: SHGs also provide a platform to discuss health, nutrition, and social issues like domestic violence — making them a vehicle for social empowerment, not just financial access.

7
What are the reasons why the banks might not be willing to lend to certain borrowers?
Answer
  • Absence of collateral: Banks require assets as security. Landless labourers, small farmers, and urban poor often have no acceptable collateral.
  • Lack of documentation: Banks need income proof, employment records, and identity documents. Many informal workers lack these.
  • No credit history: First-time borrowers without a banking relationship cannot demonstrate creditworthiness.
  • Low and irregular income: Banks assess repayment capacity. Borrowers with uncertain or seasonal income (like daily wage workers) are seen as high risk.
  • Small loan amounts: The cost of processing and monitoring a very small loan can exceed the interest earned — making small loans unprofitable for banks.
  • Remote locations: Banks are often not present in remote rural areas, making lending impractical even if the borrower is creditworthy.
8
In what ways does the Reserve Bank of India supervise the functioning of banks? Why is this necessary?
Answer

Ways RBI supervises banks:

  • Cash reserve requirement: RBI monitors that banks maintain a minimum cash balance (about 5% of deposits) to meet withdrawal demands.
  • Priority sector lending: RBI ensures banks give loans not just to profit-making businesses but also to small farmers, small-scale industries, and small borrowers — promoting equitable development.
  • Periodic reporting: Banks must periodically submit information to RBI on how much they are lending, to whom, and at what interest rates.
  • Interest rate guidance: RBI sets benchmark rates (repo rate, etc.) that influence lending rates across the banking system.
  • Regulation of new banks: RBI issues licences to new banks and can revoke them if banks don't follow rules.

Why this is necessary:

  • Banks hold the savings of millions of ordinary depositors — without supervision, they could misuse these funds.
  • If a bank fails, millions of depositors could lose their savings — causing economic and social disruption.
  • Without direction, banks might only lend to profitable large borrowers, excluding farmers and the poor who need credit for development.
  • To maintain trust in the banking system and ensure financial stability of the entire economy.
9
Analyse the role of credit for development.
Answer

Credit plays a dual role in development — it can be a powerful enabler or a dangerous trap depending on circumstances:

Positive role (when conditions are right):

  • Salim's case: Credit helped him meet working capital needs, complete production, earn profit, and grow his business.
  • Farmers can buy seeds and fertilisers before the harvest, enabling cultivation.
  • Small businesses can expand, hire workers, and increase output.
  • Cheap and affordable credit is crucial for the country's development — enabling investment in agriculture, industry, and services.

Negative role (in high-risk situations):

  • Swapna's case: Crop failure + high-interest moneylender = debt trap, loss of land, worse-off than before.
  • High-cost informal credit increases debt burden, reduces income available for families, and can perpetuate poverty.

Conclusion: The role of credit in development depends on: the interest rate (affordable vs exploitative), the purpose (productive vs consumption in crisis), the risk level, and the presence of support systems like insurance and formal banking access.

Choose the most appropriate: (i) In a SHG, decisions are taken by? (ii) Formal sources of credit does not include?
Answers
  • (i) (b) Members — In a SHG, most important decisions about savings and loan activities are taken by the group members themselves. The group decides on purpose of loans, amount, interest rate, and repayment schedule democratically.
  • (ii) (c) Employers — Formal sources of credit include banks and cooperatives, which are supervised by RBI. Employers lending money are an informal source of credit — unregulated, often at high interest rates.

Important Terms to Know

Double Coincidence of Wants

In barter, when what a seller wants to sell is exactly what the buyer wants to buy, AND the buyer has what the seller needs simultaneously. Very hard to arrange.

Medium of Exchange

Money's key role — acts as an intermediate step in transactions, separating the act of selling from buying and eliminating double coincidence of wants.

Currency

Paper notes and coins that constitute modern money. Issued by RBI in India on behalf of the Central Government. No intrinsic value but legally mandated.

Demand Deposits

Bank deposits that can be withdrawn on demand at any time. Along with currency, they constitute money in the modern economy. Enable cheque payments.

Cheque

A paper instructing the bank to pay a specific amount from the payer's account to the person named. Allows cashless payment from demand deposits.

Credit (Loan)

An agreement where the lender supplies the borrower with money, goods, or services in return for the promise of future payment with interest.

Collateral

An asset owned by the borrower (land, building, vehicle, livestock, bank deposits) pledged as guarantee for a loan. Lender can sell it if loan is not repaid.

Terms of Credit

The conditions of a loan: interest rate + collateral and documentation requirements + mode of repayment. Vary significantly between formal and informal lenders.

Debt Trap

When a borrower cannot repay a loan and must borrow more to repay the old loan, getting progressively deeper into debt. Swapna's case is the classic example.

Formal Sector Credit

Loans from banks and cooperatives — supervised by RBI, lower interest rates, require collateral and documentation. Accessible mainly to those with assets.

Informal Sector Credit

Loans from moneylenders, traders, employers, relatives — no supervision, very high interest rates (up to 60%+ p.a.), no collateral needed but no legal protection.

Reserve Bank of India (RBI)

India's central bank. Issues currency on behalf of the government. Supervises formal credit — monitors cash balance, priority sector lending, and interest rates.

Self-Help Group (SHG)

15-20 rural poor (mainly women) pool savings monthly, lend to members at reasonable rates, and after 1-2 years access bank loans as a group without individual collateral.

Grameen Bank

Bangladesh's pioneering microfinance institution (founded 1970s). Over 9 million mostly-women members in 81,600 villages by 2018. Proves poor women are reliable borrowers.

Cooperative Society (Credit)

Members pool resources; deposits are used as collateral to get bank loans; loans given to members for agriculture, housing, and other needs at reasonable rates.

Important Facts for Exams
  • In India, currency notes are issued by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) on behalf of the Central Government. No other individual or organisation can issue currency.
  • Banks in India hold about 5% of their deposits as cash and use the rest to extend loans.
  • Banks make money by charging a higher interest rate on loans than they pay on deposits — the difference is their main income.
  • As of 2019 (NSSO survey), commercial banks provide 51% of rural credit. Moneylenders provide 23%.
  • 54% of poor urban households' loans are from informal sources vs only 17% for rich households (Graph 2).
  • A typical SHG has 15-20 members, usually from the same neighbourhood, saving ₹25-100/month each.
  • Grameen Bank was founded in the 1970s by Professor Muhammad Yunus (2006 Nobel Peace Prize winner) in Bangladesh. By 2018: 9 million+ members in 81,600 villages.
  • Salim = positive credit story (working capital → delivered order → repaid). Swapna = negative credit story (crop failure → debt trap → sold land).
  • Collateral is the key requirement for formal sector loans. Its absence is why the poor cannot access cheap bank credit.
  • SHGs overcome the collateral problem — banks lend to the group, the group's collective responsibility replaces individual collateral.
  • Arun (Sonpur, 7 acres) borrows from bank at 8.5% p.a. Shyamal (1.5 acres) pays 36% p.a. to trader. Rama (landless) pays 60% p.a. to employer.
  • RBI supervises formal lending — monitors cash balance, priority sector lending, and periodic reporting by all banks.
